# Legal Status of AI-Generated Content Copyright (US, EU, UK, Japan, China) — March 2026

## Executive Summary

**Your studio's 90% AI-generated game assets face a serious copyright protection gap in most jurisdictions.** As of March 2026, purely AI-generated content (prompt-only, no meaningful human modification) is **uncopyrightable in the US** (Supreme Court denied certiorari in *Thaler v. Perlmutter*, March 2, 2026), **unprotected in the EU** (human authorship required; EU Parliament Report A10-0019/2026 reaffirms this), **at risk of losing protection in the UK** (government proposes repealing CDPA §9(3)), **unprotected in Japan** unless human creative expression is demonstrated, and **potentially protectable in China** only if you document extensive human creative input (Beijing Internet Court precedent). The path forward is restructuring your pipeline so humans make demonstrable creative decisions at every stage. **Confidence: 75%** (law is actively evolving in all 5 jurisdictions; legislative changes could shift within 12-18 months).

## Key Findings

1. **US (settled as of March 2026):** Supreme Court denied certiorari in *Thaler v. Perlmutter* on March 2, 2026, confirming the D.C. Circuit ruling that AI cannot be an "author" under the Copyright Act. The US Copyright Office Part 2 Report (Jan 29, 2025) confirms: prompting alone is insufficient; only works with "meaningful human authorship" (selection, arrangement, modification of AI outputs) qualify. First successful AI-assisted registration: Invoke's image (2025), where human "actively selected, coordinated, and arranged" elements. ([SCOTUSblog](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/case-files/thaler-v-perlmutter/), [USCO Report](https://www.copyright.gov/ai/Copyright-and-Artificial-Intelligence-Part-2-Copyrightability-Report.pdf), [Holland & Knight](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/03/the-final-word-supreme-court-refuses-to-hear-case-on-ai-authorship))

2. **EU (evolving, stricter):** EU Copyright Directive (2019/790) Article 4 allows TDM with opt-out; EU AI Act (effective Aug 2, 2025 for GPAI obligations) requires training data transparency and copyright compliance. EU Parliament Report A10-0019/2026 (Feb 25, 2026) reaffirms human authorship requirement for outputs and proposes rebuttable presumption of infringement if AI providers lack training transparency. AI-generated outputs without human authorship are NOT copyrightable under EU law. ([EU Parliament A10-0019/2026](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/doceo/document/A-10-2026-0019_EN.html), [Clifford Chance](https://www.cliffordchance.com/insights/resources/blogs/ip-insights/2025/10/copyright-compliance-under-the-eu-ai-act-for-gpai-model-providers.html))

3. **UK (in flux — protection may be removed):** CDPA 1988 §9(3) uniquely provides 50-year copyright for "computer-generated works" where "there is no human author." However, in March 2026 the UK Government proposed repealing §9(3), concluding that AI-only works should not be protected. If repealed, UK aligns with US/EU. Currently, §9(3) remains law but its future is uncertain. ([Authors Alliance](https://www.authorsalliance.org/2025/05/19/the-uks-curious-case-of-copyright-for-ai-generated-works-what-section-93-means-today/), [HSF Kramer](https://www.hsfkramer.com/notes/ip/2026-03/uk-government-report-on-copyright-and-ai-concludes-more-evidence-is-needed-although-s9-3-cdpa-could-go))

4. **Japan (no protection without creative expression):** Under Japan's Copyright Act, a "work" requires "creative expression of thoughts or sentiments" (Article 2). AI-generated content without human creative contribution is not a copyrightable work. The Agency of Cultural Affairs published "Checklist & Guidance on AI and Copyright" (July 31, 2025). The AI Promotion Act (May 28, 2025) promotes AI but does not create new copyright protections for AI outputs. ([Cultural Affairs Agency](https://www.bunka.go.jp/english/policy/copyright/pdf/94055801_01.pdf), [Chambers Japan AI 2025](https://practiceguides.chambers.com/practice-guides/artificial-intelligence-2025/japan))

5. **China (most favorable, but conditional):** Beijing Internet Court (Nov 2023, affirmed Sep 2025) grants copyright to AI-generated images IF the human user demonstrates "intellectual creation" through iterative prompting, parameter adjustment, and selection. Documentation of creative process is crucial. Changshu People's Court (Mar 2025) extended this to Midjourney + Photoshop refinement. However, Beijing Internet Court (Sep 2025) also requires evidence of "personalized expression." China is currently the most favorable jurisdiction for AI-generated content copyright, but requires documented human creative input. ([Kluwer Copyright Blog](https://legalblogs.wolterskluwer.com/copyright-blog/beijing-internet-court-grants-copyright-to-ai-generated-image-for-the-first-time/), [DLA Piper](https://www.dlapiper.com/en-at/insights/publications/2025/05/another-chinese-court-finds-that-ai-generated-images-can-be-protected-by-copyright), [Linklaters](https://techinsights.linklaters.com/post/102lo55/china-documentation-of-creative-processes-is-crucial-to-claim-copyright-in-ai-ge))

## Implementation Guidance

### Restructure Your Pipeline for Copyrightability

The goal: transform "AI-generated assets" into "human-authored works created with AI assistance."

**Tier 1: Concept & Direction (Human)**
- Human artists create concept art, mood boards, style guides
- Human writes detailed creative briefs (not just prompts)
- Document: creative intent, reference materials, artistic decisions

**Tier 2: AI Generation (Tool)**
- Use AI to generate candidate assets from human creative direction
- Generate multiple variants (10-20 per asset)
- Document: all prompts, parameters, model versions, timestamps

**Tier 3: Human Selection & Modification (Critical for Copyright)**
- Human artist selects from candidates based on creative judgment
- Human modifies, composites, paints over, adjusts color/composition
- Minimum threshold: demonstrable creative choices that shape final output
- Document: before/after comparisons, modification logs, decision rationale

**Tier 4: Integration & Arrangement (Human)**
- Human places assets in game world with creative arrangement
- Level design, lighting, composition = human authorship
- The game as a compiled whole is a copyrightable compilation

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument 1: "The compilation copyright protects the game regardless of individual asset copyrightability"

**Evidence for:** Under US law (17 USC §103), compilations — creative selection and arrangement of elements — are copyrightable even if individual elements are not. A game's overall design, level layout, narrative, and UI arrangement constitute a protectable compilation.

**Rebuttal:** Partially valid. The compilation copyright protects the arrangement, not the individual assets. A competitor could extract and reuse individual AI-generated textures, models, or sounds without infringing the compilation copyright — they just can't copy the exact same arrangement. For a game where visual assets ARE the product (e.g., asset-heavy genres), this is insufficient protection.

### Counterargument 2: "AI copyright law will inevitably expand to protect AI outputs — we should wait"

**Evidence for:** China is trending toward protection. EU Parliament report acknowledges the issue. AI lobby groups push for expanded protection. Economic incentives favor eventual protection.

**Rebuttal:** Dangerous bet. US Supreme Court just denied certiorari (March 2, 2026) — this is settled law for at least 5-10 years absent Congressional action. No AI copyright bill has passed committee in the US Congress. EU Parliament Report A10-0019/2026 explicitly reaffirms human authorship. UK is moving to REMOVE existing protection. Waiting is the highest-risk strategy.

### Counterargument 3: "Our AI tools' TOS grant us ownership of outputs, so we have copyright"

**Evidence for:** Most AI tool TOS (Midjourney, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion) assign output rights to the user.

**Rebuttal:** TOS cannot create copyright where copyright law says none exists. If the output lacks human authorship, it is in the public domain regardless of what the TOS says. TOS only assigns whatever rights exist — if those rights are zero, you get zero. This is a fundamental misunderstanding that persists in the industry.
