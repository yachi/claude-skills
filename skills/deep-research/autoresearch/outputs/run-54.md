# 50 MGD Seawater Desalination Plant in Southern California: Permitting Strategy and Lessons from Poseidon Huntington Beach

## Executive Summary

A 50 MGD seawater desalination plant in southern California is technically and financially feasible at $1.2B, but permitting success depends on avoiding the specific failures that killed Poseidon Huntington Beach (denied 11-0 by the California Coastal Commission in May 2022). The three fatal flaws were: (1) open ocean intake killing marine life, (2) inadequate brine mitigation, and (3) lack of demonstrated local water demand. Your plant must use **subsurface intakes** per Ocean Plan Section III.M.2, achieve **brine salinity ≤2.0 ppt above ambient within 100m** per Section III.M.3, and demonstrate demand through integration with a regional water supply portfolio. Delivered water cost will be approximately **$2,800-3,400/acre-foot** based on Carlsbad precedent. **Confidence level: 72%** that a well-designed project can obtain permits within 5-7 years.

## Key Findings

1. **Poseidon Huntington Beach was denied 11-0 on May 12, 2022** after 20+ years of development. Key reasons: non-compliance with California Coastal Act, marine life mortality from open ocean intake (100M gallons/day seawater withdrawal), inadequate brine discharge mitigation, and lack of demonstrated local water demand in northern Orange County with ample groundwater ([CalMatters](https://calmatters.org/environment/2022/05/california-desalination-plant-coastal-commission/); [Voice of OC](https://voiceofoc.org/2022/05/3153068/)).

2. **Ocean Plan Desalination Amendment (Resolution 2015-0033, Section III.M)** requires all new seawater desalination facilities to use best available site, design, and technology to minimize marine life mortality. Subsurface intakes are preferred unless Regional Water Board determines infeasibility. Brine discharge must not exceed 2.0 ppt above natural background within 100m of discharge point ([SWRCB Ocean Plan](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/water_issues/programs/ocean/desalination/); [SWRCB Resolution 2015-0033](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/board_decisions/adopted_orders/resolutions/2015/rs2015_0033_sr050615.pdf)).

3. **Carlsbad Desalination Plant (50 MGD, operational since December 2015)** cost ~$1B for plant + pipelines. Current delivered cost is ~$3,400/acre-foot (FY 2024), up from $2,131-2,367/acre-foot in original contract. $200M intake/discharge facility upgrade completed December 2024 to comply with Ocean Plan Amendment. Consumes 3,500 kWh/acre-foot ([Wikipedia — Carlsbad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_%22Bud%22_Lewis_Carlsbad_Desalination_Plant); [ENR](https://www.enr.com/articles/61752-best-water-environment-carlsbad-desalination-plant-intake-and-discharge-facility)).

4. **California Coastal Act Section 30260** provides override for coastal-dependent industrial facilities if: (a) alternative locations are infeasible or more environmentally damaging, (b) denial would adversely affect public welfare, and (c) adverse environmental effects are mitigated to maximum extent feasible. SB 704 (signed September 30, 2023) strengthens technical review for desalination projects ([California Public Resources Code Section 30260](https://california.public.law/codes/ca_pub_res_code_section_30260); [SB 704 text](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240SB704)).

5. **NPDES permit for brine discharge** is issued by Regional Water Quality Control Board. Brine from 50 MGD RO plant at ~50% recovery = ~25 MGD brine at ~65-70 ppt salinity. Multiport diffuser is best available technology for achieving 2.0 ppt limit at 100m mixing zone ([SWRCB Brine Management](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/water_issues/programs/ocean/desalination/docs/dpr.pdf)).

6. **Energy is 40-60% of OPEX.** At 3-4 kWh/m³ for modern SWRO and California commercial electricity rates (~$0.15-0.22/kWh), energy cost alone is $0.45-0.88/m³ ($555-1,085/acre-foot). Total OPEX: $18-40M/year for 50 MGD ([Genesis Water Tech](https://genesiswatertech.com/blog-post/seawater-ro-operating-costs-analysis/)).

7. **Ocean Plan amendments are under active revision.** SWRCB accepted comments through November 13, 2024 on scope of general amendments to seawater desalination provisions. Next revision will consider recommendations from the Seawater Desalination Siting and Streamlining Report ([SWRCB Ocean Standards](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/water_issues/programs/ocean/desalination/)).

8. **UC Davis comparative study of four California desalination projects** (Carlsbad, Huntington Beach, Monterey, Doheny) identifies key success factors: co-location with existing infrastructure, subsurface intakes, demonstrated regional demand, and phased environmental mitigation ([UC Davis Environs](https://environs.law.ucdavis.edu/sites/g/files/dgvnsk15356/files/2025-05/Huang%20-%20Macroed.pdf)).

```python
import numpy as np

# Desalination plant financial model: 50 MGD SWRO in Southern California
np.random.seed(42)
n_sims = 10_000

# CAPEX
capex = np.random.uniform(850e6, 1.23e9, n_sims)

# Annual production (acre-feet)
capacity_af = 56_000  # 50 MGD
utilization = np.random.uniform(0.85, 0.95, n_sims)
annual_production = capacity_af * utilization

# OPEX per acre-foot
energy_cost = np.random.uniform(550, 750, n_sims)
membrane_cost = np.random.uniform(120, 200, n_sims)
other_opex = np.random.uniform(150, 280, n_sims)
opex_per_af = energy_cost + membrane_cost + other_opex

# Revenue (water sale price per acre-foot)
water_price = np.random.uniform(2500, 4000, n_sims)  # Comparable to imported water

# Permitting delay (years before revenue starts)
permit_delay = np.random.choice([4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 10], n_sims, p=[0.10, 0.25, 0.30, 0.20, 0.10, 0.05])
construction = 3  # years

discount_rate = 0.05  # Municipal bond rate
project_life = 30

npv = np.zeros(n_sims)
for i in range(n_sims):
    cashflows = [-capex[i]]
    total_delay = int(permit_delay[i]) + construction
    for yr in range(1, total_delay + project_life + 1):
        if yr <= total_delay:
            cashflows.append(-10e6)  # Permitting/construction costs
        else:
            revenue = annual_production[i] * water_price[i]
            opex = annual_production[i] * opex_per_af[i]
            cashflows.append(revenue - opex)
    npv[i] = sum(cf / (1 + discount_rate)**t for t, cf in enumerate(cashflows))

print("50 MGD SWRO Financial Model (10,000 simulations)")
print("=" * 55)
print(f"Mean NPV:              ${npv.mean()/1e6:>10,.0f}M")
print(f"Median NPV:            ${np.median(npv)/1e6:>10,.0f}M")
print(f"P(NPV > 0):            {(npv > 0).mean():>10.1%}")
print(f"Mean delivered cost:    ${opex_per_af.mean():>10,.0f}/acre-foot (OPEX only)")
print(f"Mean permit delay:      {permit_delay.mean():>10.1f} years")
print(f"Mean utilization:       {utilization.mean():>10.1%}")
```

### Critical Success Factors (Poseidon Lessons Applied)

1. **Subsurface intake — non-negotiable.** Use slant wells (Doheny model) or infiltration galleries. This eliminates CWA §316(b) impingement/entrainment entirely and satisfies Ocean Plan Section III.M.2 preference. Budget $150-250M.

2. **Brine discharge via multiport diffuser.** Design for 2.0 ppt limit at 100m per Section III.M.3. Co-mingle with treated wastewater effluent for additional dilution. Model brine plume dispersion using CORMIX or Visual Plumes software.

3. **Demonstrate water demand.** This killed Poseidon — northern OC had ample groundwater. Your project must integrate with a regional water agency (e.g., Metropolitan Water District, San Diego County Water Authority) that can demonstrate a supply-demand gap.

4. **Early CCC engagement.** Per SB 704, CCC now receives technical advice on desalination. Submit pre-application consultation request at least 18 months before formal application.

5. **Community engagement from Day 1.** Poseidon faced organized opposition (Stop Poseidon Coalition). Budget $5-10M for community relations, public workshops, and local benefit agreements.

6. **Co-locate with existing infrastructure.** Carlsbad succeeded partly because it co-located with Encina Power Station. Site near existing outfall, power, and distribution infrastructure.

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument 1: "Desalination is too expensive compared to alternatives"
At $2,780/acre-foot, seawater desal is 50-100% more expensive than imported water or potable reuse. However, it is drought-proof — the only supply source completely independent of precipitation. California's imported water supply faces structural decline (Colorado River cuts, Bay-Delta restrictions). A portfolio approach combining desal (drought insurance) + DPR (cost-effective) + conservation is optimal. The question is not desal vs. alternatives but what percentage of the portfolio should be desal.

### Counterargument 2: "The $1.2B budget is insufficient"
Carlsbad cost ~$1B (2012 dollars) for 50 MGD with open ocean intake. Subsurface intake adds $100-200M. In 2026 dollars with construction inflation (~30% since 2012), a realistic budget is $1.3-1.5B. The $1.2B budget is tight and may require value engineering or phased construction (25 MGD Phase 1, expandable to 50 MGD). Budget risk is real.

### Counterargument 3: "CCC will deny any desalination project after Poseidon"
The CCC approved the Doheny desalination project (South Coast Water District) in October 2022, just months after denying Poseidon. The difference: Doheny uses subsurface intake, has demonstrated local demand, and had community support. The CCC is not anti-desal — it is anti-bad-desal. A compliant project has a viable path.

## Recommendation

**Proceed with 50 MGD SWRO plant development, but with three non-negotiable conditions:**

1. **Subsurface intake only.** Open ocean intake is a permit-killer after Poseidon. Budget $150-250M for slant wells or infiltration galleries.
2. **Secure a regional water agency partner** (MWD, SDCWA, or equivalent) that can formally document a supply-demand gap justifying desal in the portfolio.
3. **Consider phased construction** (25 MGD Phase 1) to fit within $1.2B and reduce permitting risk.

**Conditions under which this recommendation changes:**
- If subsurface intake proves geologically infeasible at the selected site → pivot to screened intake with comprehensive marine life mitigation (increases risk, adds $50-100M in mitigation)
- If Ocean Plan amendments (under revision) impose stricter brine limits → may require zero-liquid discharge, adding $200-400M
- If potable reuse (DPR) regulations finalize favorably in California → DPR may be more cost-effective; reduce desal capacity and increase DPR
- If Colorado River allocation improves substantially → reduces urgency of drought-proof supply
- If construction cost escalation exceeds 20% over budget → defer to Phase 1 (25 MGD) only

**Confidence: 72%** — The Carlsbad and Doheny precedents demonstrate that California desalination can be permitted, but only with subsurface intake, demonstrated demand, and comprehensive environmental mitigation. The permitting timeline and budget are the primary uncertainties.
