# Indigenous Land Rights and Lithium Mining: ILO 169, FPIC, and ESG Compliance in the Lithium Triangle

## Executive Summary

A European EV battery manufacturer seeks to secure lithium carbonate supply through a joint venture with a mining company operating brine extraction on the Salar de Atacama (Chile) and Salar del Hombre Muerto (Argentina), both within ancestral territories of Atacameno/Lickanantay and Diaguita-Calchaqui indigenous communities. The legal and ESG frameworks governing this engagement are layered and stringent: ILO Convention 169 (Articles 6 and 15) mandates consultation with the objective of achieving consent; UNDRIP Article 32 requires FPIC before any mineral extraction on indigenous territories; and the EU Critical Raw Materials Act (Regulation 2024/1252) imposes supply-chain due diligence including indigenous rights verification for strategic raw materials entering the EU market. Despite these frameworks, implementation gaps remain pervasive — virtually no lithium operation in the Lithium Triangle has obtained genuine FPIC to date. The recommended approach: do not proceed with the JV until a credible FPIC process is designed and executed, with independent verification. **Confidence: 80%** on legal requirements; moderate uncertainty on enforceability in practice given the gap between law and implementation in the region.

## Key Findings

1. **ILO Convention 169 Article 15** requires governments to consult indigenous peoples before permitting mineral exploration/exploitation on their lands, with the objective of "ascertaining whether and to what degree their interests would be prejudiced" — and the peoples concerned "shall wherever possible participate in the benefits" ([ILO Convention 169 Text](https://www.ilo.org/media/458651/download))
2. **Only 24 countries have ratified ILO 169** — but critically, Argentina (2000), Chile (2008), and Bolivia (1991) are all among them, making it domestically binding law in all three Lithium Triangle countries ([Cultural Survival](https://www.culturalsurvival.org/news/after-30-years-only-23-countries-have-ratified-indigenous-and-tribal-peoples-convention-ilo))
3. **No lithium project in the Lithium Triangle has obtained genuine FPIC** according to FIDH/IWGIA reporting — consultations have been procedurally deficient, with communities left unaware of compensation entitlements and excluded from benefit-sharing ([FIDH Report](https://www.fidh.org/en/issues/business-human-rights-environment/lithium-triangle-scramble-transition-minerals-endangers-human-rights-south-america))
4. **EU CRMA (Regulation 2024/1252) entered into force May 2024** — designates lithium as a strategic raw material and requires sustainability due diligence including respect for UNDRIP, ILO 169, and UN Guiding Principles for strategic project certification ([EU CRMA](https://single-market-economy.ec.europa.eu/sectors/raw-materials/areas-specific-interest/critical-raw-materials/critical-raw-materials-act_en))
5. **Lithium carbonate prices expected to reach $12,000-$28,000/ton in 2026** depending on supply-demand balance; water-intensive brine extraction threatens the very ecosystems that sustain indigenous livelihoods ([Investing News](https://investingnews.com/daily/resource-investing/battery-metals-investing/lithium-investing/lithium-forecast/))
6. **Chile's landmark lithium governance model (2026)** attempts to share the lithium windfall with indigenous communities but has fractured communities over terms and representation ([Climate Change News](https://www.climatechangenews.com/2026/03/18/landmark-deal-to-share-chiles-lithium-windfall-fractures-indigenous-communities/))

## Implementation Guidance

### Step 1: Pre-Engagement Assessment (Months 1-3)

1. **Map affected communities**: Identify all indigenous communities with ancestral or legal claims to the concession areas
   - Chile: Atacameno/Lickanantay communities (Salar de Atacama)
   - Argentina: Diaguita-Calchaqui communities (Salar del Hombre Muerto)
2. **Engage independent facilitator** experienced in FPIC processes in Latin American mining contexts (not the mining company's own community relations team)
3. **Commission participatory EIA** per ILO 169 Art. 7(3) — environmental, social, spiritual, and cultural dimensions
4. **Assess water rights** — quantify current indigenous water use and model extraction impacts on aquifer levels

### Step 2: FPIC Process Design (Months 3-6)

1. Design process jointly with community-designated representatives (not government-appointed leaders)
2. Ensure **Free**: No coercion; communities can say no without consequence
3. Ensure **Prior**: Before any ground disturbance or binding commercial agreements
4. Ensure **Informed**: Full disclosure in indigenous languages of:
   - Extraction volumes and methods
   - Water table impact projections (with uncertainty ranges)
   - Revenue projections and proposed benefit-sharing formula
   - Environmental remediation plans
   - Duration and reversibility
5. Ensure **Consent**: Binary right to approve or reject — not just "consultation"

### Step 3: Benefit-Sharing Agreement (Months 6-18)

1. Propose benefit-sharing at 3-5% of gross revenue (Chile benchmark)
2. Include: direct payments, infrastructure investment, education/health programs, cultural preservation
3. Structure payments through community-controlled trust (not company-administered)
4. Include escalation clauses tied to lithium price increases
5. Include termination rights if environmental commitments are breached

### Step 4: EU CRMA Compliance (Ongoing)

1. Register project as "strategic" under CRMA if seeking EU preferential treatment
2. Prepare sustainability certification per CRMA Art. 5-6 criteria
3. Obtain IRMA or equivalent third-party certification
4. Report in CSRD annual sustainability report: FPIC process, benefit-sharing, water impacts

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument 1: "FPIC gives indigenous communities an unreasonable veto over national economic development and the clean energy transition"
**Rebuttal**: This framing mischaracterizes both the law and the economics. ILO 169 Art. 6 requires consultation "with the objective of achieving agreement or consent" — the obligation is to negotiate in good faith, not to accept an unconditional veto. In practice, communities rarely reject projects outright; they reject projects that offer no benefit-sharing, no environmental safeguards, and no genuine consultation. The real cost of skipping FPIC is not the cost of compliance (~3.4% of revenue) but the cost of opposition: project delays ($25M+), litigation, ESG downgrades, and potential loss of EU market access under CRMA. SQM's operations in Atacama demonstrate that projects CAN proceed with indigenous engagement — the question is whether the engagement is genuine or performative. Counterargument rejected.

### Counterargument 2: "The clean energy transition requires lithium urgently — ESG requirements slow down critical supply"
**Rebuttal**: This counterargument has partial merit. EU lithium demand is projected to grow 12x by 2030 ([EU CRMA](https://single-market-economy.ec.europa.eu/sectors/raw-materials/areas-specific-interest/critical-raw-materials/critical-raw-materials-act_en)), and supply bottlenecks are real. However, the framing creates a false trade-off: FPIC processes take 18-24 months while mine development takes 5-10 years — the rights compliance is not the bottleneck. Projects that skip indigenous rights face longer delays from legal challenges (Thacker Pass litigation delayed operations by 2+ years). The faster path to market goes through indigenous consent, not around it.

### Counterargument 3: "Water impact from brine extraction is overstated — the evaporation ponds use brine, not freshwater"
**Rebuttal**: While brine is technically distinct from freshwater, the hydrogeological reality is that brine extraction lowers the water table, which draws down connected freshwater aquifers. The Salar de Atacama has lost an estimated 30-40% of its freshwater inflow since large-scale lithium extraction began ([OHCHR Chile Report](https://www.ohchr.org/en/stories/2026/02/resisting-age-lithium-right-healthy-environment-indigenous-territories-chile)). The Atacameno communities depend on these aquifers for drinking water, agriculture, and pastoral activities. Direct Lithium Extraction (DLE) technology, which returns brine to the salar after lithium extraction, reduces but does not eliminate this impact. The counterargument is factually misleading.

## Recommendation

**Do not proceed with the JV until a credible FPIC process is designed, executed, and documented.** The 18-24 month FPIC timeline is shorter than the mine development timeline and is therefore NOT on the critical path.

**Immediate actions:**
1. **Hire an independent FPIC facilitator** with Latin American mining experience ($500,000)
2. **Commission participatory EIA** including hydrological modeling ($800,000-1.2M)
3. **Begin community mapping** — identify all affected communities and their legitimate representative institutions
4. **Engage EU CRMA compliance counsel** to ensure the FPIC process meets Art. 5-6 certification requirements
5. **Evaluate DLE technology** as a water-impact mitigation option for the JV ($2-5M feasibility study)

**Budget: $1.7M upfront FPIC + $14.1M/yr ongoing compliance = ~3.4% of revenue**
**Non-compliance risk: $140M+ in litigation, delays, ESG damage, and EU market access loss**

**Overall confidence: 80%** — legal frameworks are clear and binding in all three Lithium Triangle countries; uncertainty centers on enforcement consistency and whether community negotiations will achieve agreement within projected timelines.

**Conditions that change this recommendation:**
- If communities reject the project after genuine FPIC: respect the decision; pivot to Australian or US sources
- If DLE proves commercially viable at scale: reduces water impact argument significantly but does not eliminate land rights obligations
- If EU CRMA enforcement proves weak: reduces market-access risk but does not eliminate legal or reputational risk
- If lithium prices fall below $8,000/t: project economics may not support 3-5% benefit-sharing; renegotiate terms
