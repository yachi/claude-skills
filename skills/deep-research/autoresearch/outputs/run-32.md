# DTC Whole Genome Sequencing with AI Health Risk Scores: "Better Than Your Doctor"

## Executive Summary

This proposal faces a regulatory minefield that makes the "better than your doctor" marketing claim not just inadvisable but potentially illegal under FTC Section 5, FDA SaMD regulations, and state consumer protection laws. **The premise that AI-interpreted WGS health risk scores are "better than your doctor" is flawed on three independent grounds:** (1) No DTC polygenic risk score product has received FDA clearance ([AHA/Circulation](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001304)); (2) A systematic review of 591 studies found zero demonstrating clinical utility of PRS, though 22 showed clinical validity ([CDC Genomics Blog](https://blogs.cdc.gov/genomics/2022/07/05/polygenic-risk-scores/)); (3) PRS accuracy drops sharply in non-European populations, creating liability for disparate health outcomes ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7013150/)). 23andMe — the category pioneer — filed for bankruptcy in March 2025 with its genetic data of 15M users at risk of sale ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/24/nx-s1-5338622/23andme-bankruptcy-genetic-data-privacy)). The viable path is a physician-mediated model with AI as a clinical decision support tool (not a replacement), requiring FDA SaMD authorization, CLIA certification, HIPAA compliance, and abandoning the "better than your doctor" claim entirely. **Confidence: 85%** that the current concept as described would face regulatory enforcement; **72%** that a restructured physician-mediated model is commercially viable.

## Premise Challenge: "Better Than Your Doctor" Is Legally and Scientifically Untenable

### Legal Problem

The FTC requires that health claims be supported by "competent and reliable scientific evidence" ([FTC Health Claims](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/topics/truth-advertising/health-claims)). The claim "better than your doctor" is a comparative superiority claim that would require randomized controlled trial evidence showing that AI-interpreted WGS outperforms physician judgment across the full spectrum of health conditions. No such evidence exists. The FTC has enforcement authority including civil penalties, injunctions, and mandatory corrective advertising ([FTC Advertising & Marketing](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/advertising-marketing)).

### Scientific Problem

The ACMG points-to-consider statement on PRS clinical application states that PRS should not be used as standalone diagnostic tools and should be interpreted in the context of clinical risk factors ([ACMG/Genetics in Medicine](https://www.gimjournal.org/article/S1098-3600(23)00816-X/fulltext)). The CDC's Office of Genomics concluded in 2022 that "we are not ready to incorporate PRS into clinical settings" due to lack of clinical utility evidence ([CDC](https://blogs.cdc.gov/genomics/2022/07/05/polygenic-risk-scores/)).

### Market Precedent Problem

23andMe — the most well-funded DTC genomics company at $1.5B+ raised — filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in March 2025 ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/24/nx-s1-5338622/23andme-bankruptcy-genetic-data-privacy)). The company was sold for just $305M, and its data practices drew scrutiny from multiple state AGs ([Scientific American](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/23andme-bankruptcy-leaves-troves-of-genetic-data-at-risk/)).

## Key Findings

1. **WGS consumer cost has dropped to $249-$399** (Nebula Genomics at $249 for 30x WGS) ([BioSpace](https://www.biospace.com/prophase-labs-nebula-genomics-subsidiary-introduces-lowest-ever-ngs-pricing-of-249-for-its-whole-genome-sequencing-dna-test))
2. **No DTC PRS product has FDA clearance** — this is a regulatory gap, not an oversight ([AHA](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001304))
3. **AI health scoring is SaMD under FDA framework** — any software providing disease risk assessment meets the International Medical Device Regulators Forum (IMDRF) SaMD definition ([FDA AI/ML SaMD](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/software-medical-device-samd/artificial-intelligence-software-medical-device))
4. **FDA's LDT final rule was rescinded** on September 19, 2025, after a federal court vacated it, creating ongoing regulatory uncertainty ([Foley & Lardner](https://www.foley.com/insights/publications/2024/05/ldt-fda-phased-implementation-new-regulatory-requirements/), [Snell & Wilmer](https://www.swlaw.com/publication/fda-reverses-final-rule-on-ldts-a-win-for-labs-a-shift-in-regulatory-strategy/))
5. **GINA does NOT protect consumers from life/disability/LTC insurance discrimination** based on genetic results ([NIH/NHGRI](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/policy-issues/Genetic-Discrimination))
6. **PRS accuracy varies by ancestry** — scores derived from European GWAS have significantly attenuated predictive accuracy in non-European populations ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9828290/))
7. **23andMe's 2023 data breach** compromised 6M+ users' genetic data, resulting in $30M settlement ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/2025/03/24/nx-s1-5338622/23andme-bankruptcy-genetic-data-privacy))
8. **EU IVDR (Regulation 2017/746)** classifies predictive genetic tests as Class C, requiring Notified Body assessment ([EUR-Lex](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/PDF/?uri=CELEX:32017R0746), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8061679/))
9. **State genetic privacy laws are proliferating** — California GIPA, Illinois GIPA, and multiple states passed new genetic privacy bills in 2025 ([Orrick](https://www.orrick.com/en/Insights/2025/08/Navigating-Privacy-Gaps-and-New-Legal-Requirements-for-Companies-Processing-Genetic-Data), [Inside Privacy](https://www.insideprivacy.com/health-privacy/multiple-states-enact-genetic-privacy-legislation-in-a-busy-start-to-2025/))
10. **U.S. Senate Genomic Data Protection Act** tabled March 2025 signals federal genetic privacy regulation ([Inside Privacy](https://www.insideprivacy.com/united-states/u-s-federal-and-state-legislative-initiatives/u-s-senate-introduces-genomic-data-protection-act/))

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument 1: "AI is already outperforming doctors in specific diagnostic tasks — the marketing claim will eventually be true"

**Evidence:** AI has shown superhuman performance in narrow tasks: retinal disease detection (Google DeepMind, published in Nature Medicine), skin cancer classification (Stanford, Nature 2017), and specific ECG interpretation. Some argue it's only a matter of time before AI + WGS outperforms general practitioners for risk assessment.

**Rebuttal:** The evidence supports AI superiority in narrow, image-based diagnostic tasks — not in holistic health risk assessment from genomic data. PRS for common diseases has modest predictive value (AUC 0.60-0.75 for most conditions), and clinical risk factors often outperform or are additive to PRS ([PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7013150/)). Even if AI eventually surpasses physicians in genomic interpretation, making the marketing claim before having RCT evidence violates FTC substantiation requirements. The FTC enforces based on evidence at time of claim, not future potential.

### Counterargument 2: "The FDA's LDT rule was rescinded — DTC genomics is effectively unregulated"

**Evidence:** The FDA rescinded its 2024 LDT final rule on September 19, 2025, after a court vacated it ([Snell & Wilmer](https://www.swlaw.com/publication/fda-reverses-final-rule-on-ldts-a-win-for-labs-a-shift-in-regulatory-strategy/)). This creates a regulatory gap where LDTs, including some genomic tests, operate with less FDA oversight.

**Rebuttal:** The LDT rescission does NOT affect: (a) FDA's authority over SaMD — AI interpretation software is distinct from the laboratory test itself; (b) FTC jurisdiction over marketing claims; (c) state AG enforcement under consumer protection statutes; (d) CLIA certification requirements. The AI component is what triggers SaMD classification regardless of the lab test's regulatory status. Additionally, the regulatory pendulum could swing back — building a business on a regulatory gap is high-risk.

### Counterargument 3: "23andMe's failure was operational, not a market indictment — the market wants this"

**Evidence:** 23andMe raised $1.5B+, had 15M users, and proved massive consumer demand for genomic information. Their failure was driven by inability to monetize beyond one-time test purchases, plus the 2023 data breach.

**Rebuttal:** Partially valid — consumer demand exists. But 23andMe's trajectory also shows: (a) $6B peak valuation collapsed to $305M sale, demonstrating unsustainable economics; (b) the data breach and bankruptcy triggered a wave of state genetic privacy laws that increase compliance costs for all entrants; (c) the one-time purchase model doesn't support the infrastructure costs. Any new entrant must solve the business model problem (subscription revenue) and the trust problem (post-23andMe consumer skepticism about genetic data custody).

## Recommendation

**Abandon the "better than your doctor" positioning. Restructure as a physician-augmented genomic health platform.**

1. **Phase 1 (Months 1-12, $3-5M):** Establish CLIA/CAP-certified lab, HIPAA compliance, launch ancestry/traits-only DTC product (no health claims) to build customer base and sequencing volume.

2. **Phase 2 (Months 6-24, $5-15M):** FDA Pre-Submission for AI CDS tool, clinical validation study design, genetic counselor network buildout. Target FDA SaMD De Novo submission by Month 24.

3. **Phase 3 (Months 24-36, $5-10M):** Launch physician-mediated health risk product. AI generates draft report, board-certified geneticist reviews, genetic counseling for actionable findings. Marketing: "Your complete genome, interpreted by AI, reviewed by your doctor."

4. **Phase 4 (Month 36+):** If clinical validation RCTs demonstrate superiority to standard of care, consider more assertive (but still evidence-based) marketing claims.

**Total investment: $13-30M over 3 years.**

**Confidence: 85%** that the "better than your doctor" concept as described would face regulatory enforcement. **72%** that the restructured physician-mediated model is commercially viable. **40%** that any DTC genomic health company achieves sustained profitability (market structural challenge).

**This recommendation changes if:**
- FDA establishes a clear DTC PRS pathway (reduces regulatory cost/timeline by 50%)
- PRS clinical utility is demonstrated in large RCTs (enables stronger marketing claims)
- Federal genetic privacy law preempts state patchwork (reduces compliance cost)
