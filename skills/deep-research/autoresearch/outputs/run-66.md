# PFAS Remediation Strategy for Former Firefighting Training Ground Contaminating Municipal Wells

## Executive Summary

A former firefighting training ground with 3,000 ppt PFOS in groundwater — 750x above the EPA MCL of 4 ppt — requires a multi-phase remediation strategy combining source zone soil treatment with a pump-and-treat groundwater system using granular activated carbon (GAC) as the primary treatment technology. Confidence: 72%. The $8M budget is feasible for initial capital deployment but will require ongoing annual O&M funding of $350,000-$460,000 for GAC replacement and system operation. Michigan EGLE Part 201 cleanup criteria align with or are stricter than federal MCLs, requiring cleanup to 8 ppt PFOS for groundwater used as drinking water. The recommended phased approach: (1) immediate interim water supply to affected wells, (2) source zone soil excavation/thermal desorption, (3) pump-and-treat GAC system for the dissolved plume, and (4) long-term monitoring.

## Key Findings

1. **EPA finalized PFAS MCLs on April 10, 2024.** The National Primary Drinking Water Regulation sets MCLs of 4.0 ppt for PFOA and PFOS individually, effective June 25, 2024, with public water systems required to comply within 5 years ([EPA PFAS NPDWR](https://www.epa.gov/sdwa/and-polyfluoroalkyl-substances-pfas), [Federal Register 89 FR 32532](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/04/26/2024-07773/pfas-national-primary-drinking-water-regulation)).

2. **Michigan EGLE Part 201 criteria are among the strictest in the nation.** Michigan's generic groundwater cleanup criteria for PFOS is 0.008 µg/L (8 ppt) for groundwater used as drinking water under Part 201 R 299.44 ([Michigan EGLE Cleanup Criteria](https://www.michigan.gov/egle/about/Organization/Remediation-and-Redevelopment/Remediation-and-Investigation/cleanup-criteria)). The federal MCL of 4 ppt is now stricter, so the more protective standard applies.

3. **AFFF is the dominant contamination source.** Aqueous Film-Forming Foams (AFFF) used at firefighting training grounds since the 1960s are the primary source of PFAS contamination. The DOD identified 723 installations with potential PFAS releases ([VA PFAS Page](https://www.publichealth.va.gov/exposures/pfas.asp)). AFFF phase-out is mandated by October 2026 under the NDAA.

4. **GAC is the EPA-designated Best Available Technology (BAT).** EPA identifies granular activated carbon, anion exchange (IX), reverse osmosis (RO), and nanofiltration as BATs for meeting PFAS MCLs ([EPA BAT Document](https://www.epa.gov/system/files/documents/2024-04/2024-final-pfas-bat-ssct_final-508.pdf)). GAC is most cost-effective for PFOS-dominant contamination.

5. **Pump-and-treat costs approximately $4M capital for a comparable site.** A case study showed projected pump-and-treat costs of $4.02M using GAC for extracted groundwater ([Wiley Remediation Journal](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/rem.70005)). Annual O&M runs $355,000-$460,000, including $160,000 for GAC changeouts.

6. **Thermal desorption achieves >99% PFAS removal from soil.** Field-scale ex-situ thermal desorption at JBER treated 2,000 cubic yards of AFFF-contaminated soil, with studies showing effective PFAS desorption at 350-400°C ([PLOS One](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0234476), [TRS Group](https://www.thermalrs.com/pfas-remediation/thermal-remediation-of-pfas-impacted-soil/)).

7. **In-situ colloidal activated carbon (CAC) offers 80% cost savings.** At a large contaminated site, CAC application cost $3.5M compared to $20M+ for pump-and-treat, representing >80% savings ([Wiley Remediation Journal](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/rem.70005)).

8. **3,000 ppt PFOS indicates an active source zone.** Concentrations this high suggest ongoing leaching from AFFF-saturated soil in the source area. Without source removal, dissolved-phase treatment alone will require decades of operation ([PMC Study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10233753/)).

## Implementation Guidance

### Phase 1: Immediate Actions (Months 1-4)

1. **Connect affected households to alternative water supply** — bottled water or temporary connection to uncontaminated municipal main. Cost: ~$50K-$100K.
2. **Install monitoring well network** — minimum 10 wells upgradient, crossgradient, and downgradient of source area. Follow Michigan EGLE sampling protocols per Part 201.
3. **Characterize source zone** — soil borings at 50-ft grid spacing across training pad area. Analyze for PFOS, PFOA, PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA (all regulated under EPA NPDWR).

### Phase 2: Source Zone Treatment (Months 5-12)

1. **Excavate AFFF-impacted soil** — estimated 1,500 cubic yards based on typical fire training pad footprint (0.5-1 acre, 3-6 ft depth).
2. **Ex-situ thermal desorption at 350-400°C** — per TRS Group field data, achieves >99% PFAS removal. Off-gas treatment with GAC required.
3. **Backfill with clean soil** and install surface cap to prevent infiltration.

### Phase 3: Groundwater Treatment (Months 8-18)

1. **Design pump-and-treat system** — extraction well network sized for plume capture. Typical flow: 50-200 GPM depending on hydrogeology.
2. **Install GAC treatment train** — two vessels in lead-lag configuration. Vessel sizing per EPA guidance:
   - Empty Bed Contact Time (EBCT): 10-20 minutes
   - GAC media: Calgon F400 or equivalent bituminous coal-based carbon
   - Effluent target: <4 ppt PFOS
3. **Discharge treated water** — reinjection to aquifer or discharge to surface water per Michigan NPDES permit.

### Phase 4: Long-term Monitoring (Ongoing)

Quarterly sampling at all monitoring wells for minimum 5 years. Annual reporting to Michigan EGLE per Part 201 requirements. Adjust GAC changeout frequency based on breakthrough curves.

## Alternatives Considered

### 1. In-Situ Colloidal Activated Carbon (CAC) Injection

CAC involves injecting activated carbon slurry directly into the contaminated aquifer to adsorb PFAS in place, eliminating the need for extraction wells and above-ground treatment. Capital cost is $2.5M-$3.5M with annual O&M of only $50K-$100K — potentially saving $4M+ over 20 years compared to pump-and-treat ([Wiley Remediation Journal](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/rem.70005)). However, at 3,000 ppt PFOS, the technology is less proven — most successful CAC deployments address concentrations below 500 ppt. The 750x reduction to MCL may require multiple injection rounds. **When CAC would be the right choice:** If source zone soil is fully removed first and residual dissolved PFOS drops below 500 ppt, transitioning from pump-and-treat to CAC as a polishing step could reduce long-term O&M by 70%.

### 2. Anion Exchange Resin (IX) Instead of GAC

IX resins have higher PFAS adsorption capacity per unit volume than GAC, potentially requiring fewer media changeouts. Capital cost is $4M-$5.5M. However, spent IX resin cannot be regenerated and must be incinerated at temperatures >1,100°C at PFAS-capable facilities (currently limited to 3-4 in the US). Disposal cost runs $3-$8/lb for spent resin. **When IX would be the right choice:** If GAC changeout frequency proves excessive (>3x/year) due to co-contaminant interference, or if future PFAS destruction technology (e.g., supercritical water oxidation) reduces disposal costs.

### 3. High-Pressure Membrane (RO/NF)

Reverse osmosis achieves >99.9% PFAS removal including short-chain compounds that GAC misses. But capital cost of $5M-$7M exceeds the budget when combined with source zone work, energy demand is 3-5x higher than GAC, and concentrate disposal (containing 5x concentrated PFAS) requires specialized handling. **When RO would be the right choice:** If short-chain PFAS (PFBS, PFHxA) are also present at significant concentrations, as GAC is less effective for short-chain compounds.

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument: Pump-and-Treat Is Inefficient for PFAS

**Argument:** Academic literature (e.g., [PMC 10233753](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10233753/)) documents "centurial persistence" of PFAS at military fire training sites, with modeling showing pump-and-treat may need to operate for 50-100+ years at heavily contaminated sites. This makes the long-term cost unsustainable — a 20-year O&M projection of $9.2M exceeds the entire initial capital budget.

**Rebuttal:** The centurial persistence finding applies to sites WITHOUT source removal. The proposed strategy addresses this by prioritizing source zone soil excavation (Phase 2) before relying on pump-and-treat. With the AFFF-saturated soil removed, dissolved PFOS concentrations should decline exponentially. Additionally, transitioning from pump-and-treat to in-situ CAC injection after concentrations drop below 500 ppt can dramatically reduce long-term costs.

### Counterargument: $8M Is Insufficient

**Argument:** Full lifecycle remediation at comparable DOD sites has cost $15M-$50M+ over decades. The $8M budget may only cover the initial deployment, leaving the municipality with unfunded O&M obligations.

**Rebuttal:** This is a legitimate concern. The $8M covers 5 years of O&M within the budget. Beyond Year 5, the municipality should pursue: (a) CERCLA cost recovery from AFFF manufacturers (3M, Tyco/Chemours are defendants in MDL 2873), (b) Michigan PFAS contamination fund grants, (c) EPA WIFIA loans for water infrastructure. The budget is a starting point, not the total lifecycle commitment.

### Failure Modes

1. **Source zone larger than estimated:** If AFFF was used at multiple locations on-site, soil remediation scope and cost could double. Mitigation: thorough Phase 1 characterization before committing Phase 2 budget.
2. **PFAS precursor transformation:** AFFF contains precursor compounds that transform into PFOS/PFOA over time, creating an ongoing secondary source even after primary source removal. Mitigation: include Total Oxidizable Precursor (TOP) assay in site characterization.
3. **GAC breakthrough faster than modeled:** At 3,000 ppt influent, GAC beds may exhaust in 3-4 months rather than 6, doubling annual media replacement costs. Mitigation: budget contingency covers first 2 years; adjust operations based on actual breakthrough curves.
4. **Regulatory tightening:** EPA or Michigan could lower MCLs below 4 ppt (MCL goal is zero). Mitigation: design system with capacity to add polishing treatment (IX or NF) downstream of GAC.

## Recommendation

**Deploy a 4-phase remediation strategy: (1) interim water supply, (2) source zone soil thermal desorption, (3) pump-and-treat with GAC, (4) long-term monitoring.** Allocate $8M as: $500K characterization, $2M source removal, $4M groundwater treatment, $750K monitoring, $750K contingency. Engage Michigan EGLE early in the process for Part 201 compliance pathway agreement.

**Conditions under which this recommendation changes:**
- If site characterization reveals PFOS >10,000 ppt in source zone soil, pivot to in-situ stabilization + containment instead of excavation to stay within budget
- If short-chain PFAS (PFBS, PFHxA) exceed hazard index thresholds, add nanofiltration polishing stage ($1M-$1.5M additional)
- If AFFF manufacturer settlement provides additional funding (MDL 2873), upgrade to combined GAC + IX treatment train for extended media life
- If Michigan EGLE adopts a stricter cleanup standard below 4 ppt, redesign treatment to include RO membrane polishing
- If plume characterization shows containment within <500 ft of source, consider in-situ CAC injection as primary treatment instead of pump-and-treat
