# Solar Radiation Management for Small Island Nations: Governance, Liability, and Scientific Uncertainties

## Executive Summary

Your island nation (population 200K, 2m avg elevation) faces an existential climate threat that Paris Agreement targets will almost certainly not avert in time — global temperatures likely already passed 1.5C in 2024. Stratospheric aerosol injection (SAI) could reduce temperatures by 0.5-1.5C within 1-2 years of deployment at $2-18B/year globally, but it carries catastrophic risks (termination shock, precipitation disruption, ozone depletion) and exists in a governance vacuum. No international legal framework authorizes or prohibits SAI deployment. A de facto moratorium under the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD COP10, Decision X/33, 2010) exists but is non-binding. Your nation cannot unilaterally deploy SAI — the $18B/year-per-degree cost exceeds your GDP, and ~60 Global South nations are calling for a non-use agreement. The most actionable path: join the AOSIS coalition pushing for SRM governance, fund adaptation (seawalls, managed retreat), and participate in research governance to ensure SIDS have veto power over deployment decisions. Confidence: 55% (governance landscape is actively shifting; scientific uncertainties are large).

## Key Findings

1. **SAI could cool 1C for ~$18B/year** (2020 USD), but costs range $2-90B/year depending on scale and method. Unaffordable for a small island nation alone. ([SRM360](https://srm360.org/article/technical-feasibility-and-costs-of-sai/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratospheric_aerosol_injection))
2. **No international law explicitly governs SAI** — it falls between gaps in the Paris Agreement, CBD, ENMOD Convention, and UNFCCC. The closest instrument is CBD COP10 Decision X/33 (2010) which created a de facto moratorium on geoengineering deployment. ([Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/transnational-environmental-law/article/towards-a-nonuse-regime-on-solar-geoengineering-lessons-from-international-law-and-governance/83A71F8002DC88049D9575790743D3A1))
3. **UNEA-6 (Feb 2024) failed to agree on SRM governance** — Switzerland's transparency resolution was withdrawn after African Group + SIDS coalition demanded acknowledgment of non-use agreement and existing moratorium. ([Climate Change News](https://www.climatechangenews.com/2024/02/29/nations-fail-to-agree-ban-or-research-on-solar-geoengineering-regulations/))
4. **~60 Global South nations now call for SRM non-use agreement** including Fiji, Vanuatu, Mexico, Colombia, Pakistan, and the African Group (55 countries). ([Boell Foundation](https://www.boell.de/en/2024/10/29/un-science-summit-countries-call-non-use-solar-geoengineering))
5. **Termination shock is the critical risk**: abrupt cessation of SAI would cause rapid warming of 2-4C per decade — 10-20x natural warming rates — potentially fatal for ecosystems and vulnerable nations. Deployment must be sustained 50-100+ years. ([Frontiers in Climate](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/climate/articles/10.3389/fclim.2021.720312/full))
6. **SAI would alter monsoon patterns and precipitation** — models show reduced rainfall in South and Southeast Asia, potential disruption to crop yields for billions of people. SIDS in the Pacific and Caribbean could see altered cyclone patterns. ([Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/oocc/article/4/1/kgae007/7699797))
7. **1.5C likely already breached** — 2024 global mean temperature may have exceeded 1.5C above pre-industrial levels. Paris Agreement Article 2.1(a) target increasingly seen as aspirational rather than achievable. ([Legal Planet](https://legal-planet.org/2024/02/14/we-crossed-1-5-c-did-we-breach-the-paris-agreement/))
8. **UN Scientific Advisory Board issued SRM brief (2025)** calling for comprehensive international assessment integrating technical, societal, and environmental considerations. ([UN SAB](https://www.un.org/scientific-advisory-board/sites/default/files/2025-05/250403-SRM-Rev-8.pdf))

### Legal Positioning

Key legal instruments to invoke:
1. **No-harm principle** (customary international law) — any state deploying SAI with transboundary precipitation effects violates this

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument 1: "SAI is our only hope — adaptation alone condemns us"
**Argument**: At 2m average elevation and 1.5C already breached, no amount of seawalls can save the nation from 0.5-1m sea level rise. SAI is the only intervention that operates on a fast enough timescale.
**Evidence**: IPCC AR6 WG1 projects 0.43-0.84m sea level rise by 2100 under SSP2-4.5, with 2m elevation nations losing significant territory.
**Rebuttal**: The timeline argument is valid — SAI does work faster than mitigation. But SAI without governance creates dependency on external actors who may terminate deployment unilaterally (termination shock risk: 2-4C/decade warming). A 200K-population nation has zero leverage to prevent this. The more durable strategy is adaptation (which you control) + governance participation (to shape any future SRM deployment). Pursuing SAI advocacy without adaptation is the real existential gamble. ([Frontiers in Climate](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/climate/articles/10.3389/fclim.2021.720312/full))

### Counterargument 2: "The non-use movement is the real threat to SIDS"
**Argument**: By joining the 60-nation non-use coalition, SIDS are removing the one tool that could save them. Opposing SRM research delays the very knowledge needed for informed decisions.
**Evidence**: Some SIDS leaders and climate scientists argue SRM research is necessary precisely because climate-vulnerable nations need options. ([ODI](https://odi.org/en/insights/what-is-solar-radiation-modification-and-what-questions-should-sids-be-asking-about-the-governance-of-its-research-and-deployment/))
**Rebuttal**: The non-use movement distinguishes between deployment and research. Most advocates support governed research while opposing unilateral deployment. The strategic value for SIDS is leverage: by threatening non-cooperation, SIDS ensure they get a seat at the governance table rather than having SRM imposed on them by wealthy nations. The alternative — supporting permissive deployment — risks SIDS bearing side effects without compensation. ([Boell Foundation](https://www.boell.de/en/2024/10/29/un-science-summit-countries-call-non-use-solar-geoengineering))

### Counterargument 3: "Make Sunsets proves any actor can deploy SAI unilaterally"
**Argument**: The startup Make Sunsets has already released SO2 into the stratosphere selling "cooling credits." The governance question is moot — deployment is already happening.
**Evidence**: As of May 2025, Make Sunsets released ~0.1 tons of SO2 (trivial — volcanic eruptions release millions of tons). EPA sent enforcement request in April 2025. ([EPA](https://www.epa.gov/geoengineering/government-action))
**Rebuttal**: 0.1 tons of SO2 has zero measurable climate effect. Meaningful SAI requires 5-10 million tons/year, costing billions. The technical and financial barriers make unilateral deployment by non-state actors impossible at meaningful scales. State-level deployment by a major economy (US, China) is the real unilateral risk, and this is precisely why governance frameworks matter. ([SRM360](https://srm360.org/article/technical-feasibility-and-costs-of-sai/))

## Recommendation

**Do not pursue unilateral SAI deployment.** It is financially impossible ($18B/year per degree vs. your ~$800M GDP), technically beyond your capacity, and would violate the CBD COP10 Decision X/33 moratorium.

**Instead, pursue a three-track strategy:**

1. **Track 1: Aggressive adaptation** (sovereign, under your control) — $75-175M over 10 years via Green Climate Fund, World Bank IDA, bilateral donors. Seawalls for critical infrastructure, mangrove/coral restoration for natural buffers, managed retreat planning for lowest areas. Start now.

2. **Track 2: SRM governance coalition** — Join AOSIS SRM working group, support the African Group position at UNEA-7 (late 2025), push for SIDS veto power in any future governance framework. The goal is not to block SRM research but to ensure your nation has enforceable consent rights and compensation guarantees before any deployment occurs.

3. **Track 3: Loss & damage financing** — Leverage the new Loss and Damage Fund (COP28) and Paris Agreement Art. 8 to secure climate reparations funding independent of SRM.
