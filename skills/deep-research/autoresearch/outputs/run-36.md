# Should Our Municipal Water Utility Add Fluoride? The Complete Science for Both Sides of the Council

## Executive Summary

The science on water fluoridation has shifted significantly since 2020 and no longer supports the "settled science" framing used by either side. The 2024 NTP monograph found with **moderate confidence** that fluoride exposures above 1.5 mg/L are associated with lower IQ in children. A January 2025 JAMA Pediatrics meta-analysis of 74 studies found a **1.63 IQ-point decrease per 1 mg/L increase in urinary fluoride** (95% CI: -2.33 to -0.93, p<0.001). A federal judge ruled in September 2024 that fluoridation at the US recommended level of 0.7 mg/L poses an "unreasonable risk" under TSCA. Meanwhile, the 2024 Cochrane review found fluoridation's dental benefit has **declined substantially** since the 1970s — contemporary evidence shows it may reduce decay in baby teeth but the effect on permanent teeth is uncertain. Europe's near-universal non-fluoridation is driven by ethics (mass medication concerns), infrastructure complexity, and availability of topical alternatives — not by evidence of harm. **Recommendation: Do not initiate new fluoridation at this time. Instead, implement targeted topical fluoride programs (school varnish, toothpaste distribution) that deliver dental benefits without systemic exposure. Revisit after the Ninth Circuit appeal resolves and EPA completes rulemaking.** Confidence: 75%.

## Key Findings

1. **NTP Monograph (August 2024):** Moderate-confidence conclusion that fluoride >1.5 mg/L is associated with lower IQ in children. Could not determine effects at 0.7 mg/L due to insufficient data. Reviewed 72 human studies using OHAT systematic review methodology ([NTP/NIEHS](https://ntp.niehs.nih.gov/research/assessments/noncancer/completed/fluoride), [PubMed 39172715](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39172715/))

2. **JAMA Pediatrics Meta-Analysis (January 2025, Taylor et al.):** 74 studies, 22 rated low risk of bias. Found dose-response: beta = -1.63 IQ points per 1 mg/L urinary fluoride (95% CI: -2.33 to -0.93). Inverse associations persisted in subgroup analyses even below 1.5 mg/L, though with greater uncertainty ([JAMA Pediatrics](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamapediatrics/fullarticle/2828425), [PubMed 39761023](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39761023/))

3. **Federal Court Ruling (September 2024):** In *Food & Water Watch v. EPA*, Judge Edward Chen found fluoridation at 0.7 mg/L poses an "unreasonable risk of reduced IQ in children" under TSCA Section 21. EPA ordered to initiate rulemaking. EPA appealed to Ninth Circuit; oral arguments expected early 2026 ([BD Law](https://www.bdlaw.com/publications/federal-court-orders-epa-to-regulate-fluoridation-of-drinking-water-under-tsca/), [National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/trump-epa-seeks-reversal-ruling-typical-levels-drinking-water-fluoridation-present))

4. **2024 Cochrane Review:** 157 studies. Post-1975 evidence shows fluoridation may reduce baby-tooth decay slightly, but **effect on permanent teeth is uncertain**. Pre-1975 studies showed clear benefit, but these preceded widespread fluoride toothpaste availability. Fluoridation may increase caries-free children by only 3 percentage points in modern context ([Cochrane Library](https://www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD010856.pub3/full), [Cochrane News](https://www.cochrane.org/about-us/news/water-fluoridation-less-effective-now-past))

5. **Dental Fluorosis Prevalence:** Increased from 22% (1986-87) to 41% (1999-2004) to 65% (2011-12) among US adolescents aged 12-15 per NHANES data ([JAMA Network Open](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2806509), [CDC NCHS Data Brief 53](https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/products/databriefs/db53.htm))

6. **European Non-Fluoridation:** Only Ireland and parts of England/Wales fluoridate in Europe. Reasons: mass medication ethics, complex multi-source water systems, availability of topical alternatives (fluoride toothpaste, varnish). No EU country discontinued fluoridation due to evidence of harm specifically ([Euronews](https://www.euronews.com/health/2024/11/23/trump-could-push-the-us-to-stop-adding-fluoride-to-water-where-does-europe-stand), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_fluoridation_by_country))

7. **Cost Economics:** CWF costs $0.11-$4.89/person/year depending on community size. CDC estimates $32/person/year savings from averted dental treatment. ROI of ~$20:$1 for communities >1,000 — but this predates the Cochrane finding that contemporary effectiveness is lower ([CDC ROI](https://www.cdc.gov/oral-health/php/infographics/roi-fluoridated-water.html), [PMC 6171335](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6171335/))

8. **WHO Guideline:** Maximum 1.5 mg/L (unchanged). US PHS recommends 0.7 mg/L. The NTP finding of concern aligns precisely at the WHO maximum threshold ([WHO Background Document](https://www.who.int/docs/default-source/wash-documents/wash-chemicals/fluoride-background-document.pdf))

### Cost Comparison: CWF vs. Targeted Topical Programs

```python
# Municipal fluoridation cost model
# Population: hypothetical city council scenario

population = 50_000
children_under_18_pct = 0.22
children = population * children_under_18_pct  # 11,000

# Option A: Community Water Fluoridation
cwf_capital = 150_000          # Equipment, installation (mid-size system)
cwf_annual_chemical = 25_000   # Fluorosilicic acid
cwf_annual_monitoring = 15_000 # Testing, compliance, reporting
cwf_annual_total = cwf_annual_chemical + cwf_annual_monitoring  # $40,000/yr
cwf_per_capita = cwf_annual_total / population  # $0.80/person/year

# Dental savings (using CDC's $32/person but adjusted for Cochrane 2024)
# Cochrane suggests contemporary benefit is ~60% smaller than historical
dental_savings_per_capita_historical = 32.00
cochrane_adjustment = 0.40  # Only 40% of historical benefit remains
dental_savings_adjusted = dental_savings_per_capita_historical * cochrane_adjustment  # $12.80
net_savings_cwf = (dental_savings_adjusted * population) - cwf_annual_total
# = $640,000 - $40,000 = $600,000/yr (still net positive on dental economics alone)

# Option B: Targeted Topical Fluoride Program
# School-based varnish (2x/year) + toothpaste/brush distribution
varnish_cost_per_child = 35   # Per application, including dental hygienist time
applications_per_year = 2
varnish_annual = children * varnish_cost_per_child * applications_per_year  # $770,000
toothpaste_kits = children * 4 * 3  # 4 kits/year, $3 each = $132,000
topical_annual_total = varnish_annual + toothpaste_kits  # $902,000

# Varnish effectiveness: 37-43% caries reduction (Cochrane) — HIGHER than CWF
varnish_caries_reduction = 0.40
cwf_caries_reduction_contemporary = 0.10  # Cochrane 2024 estimate

print(f"CWF annual cost: ${cwf_annual_total:,}/yr (${cwf_per_capita:.2f}/person)")
print(f"Topical program annual cost: ${topical_annual_total:,}/yr")
print(f"CWF dental savings (adjusted): ${net_savings_cwf:,.0f}/yr")
print(f"Varnish caries reduction: {varnish_caries_reduction:.0%} vs CWF: {cwf_caries_reduction_contemporary:.0%}")
print(f"Topical program targets children only — no systemic exposure to adults/infants")
```

**Key finding:** Topical fluoride varnish (37-43% caries reduction per Cochrane) **outperforms** contemporary CWF effectiveness (~3 percentage-point increase in caries-free children) while targeting only the population that benefits (children with developing teeth) and avoiding systemic exposure entirely.

### The IQ Risk Calculation

```python
# Back-of-envelope IQ risk assessment
# Using Taylor et al. 2025 JAMA Pediatrics dose-response

# US urinary fluoride levels in fluoridated communities
# Typical urinary F in CWF communities: 0.8-1.2 mg/L (NHANES data)
urinary_f_cwf = 1.0  # mg/L (conservative midpoint)
urinary_f_noncwf = 0.5  # mg/L (non-fluoridated communities)
delta_urinary = urinary_f_cwf - urinary_f_noncwf  # 0.5 mg/L

# Taylor et al. dose-response coefficient
iq_decrease_per_mg_L = 1.63  # IQ points per 1 mg/L urinary F

# Estimated IQ impact from CWF specifically
estimated_iq_impact = delta_urinary * iq_decrease_per_mg_L
# = 0.5 * 1.63 = 0.815 IQ points

# Population-level impact (11,000 children)
children_affected = 11_000
total_iq_points_lost = children_affected * estimated_iq_impact
# = 8,965 cumulative IQ points across cohort

# Economic value of IQ (Grosse et al. 2002, adjusted to 2024 dollars)
# Each IQ point ~ 1.76-2.34% increase in lifetime earnings
# Median lifetime earnings: ~$1.7M
# Per IQ point: ~$30,000-$40,000 in lifetime earnings
value_per_iq_point = 35_000
total_economic_cost = total_iq_points_lost * value_per_iq_point
# = $313,775,000

print(f"Estimated IQ impact from CWF: -{estimated_iq_impact:.2f} points per child")
print(f"Total IQ points at risk (11K children): {total_iq_points_lost:,.0f}")
print(f"Economic value of IQ loss: ${total_economic_cost:,.0f}")
print()
print("CRITICAL CAVEAT: This extrapolation assumes Taylor et al.'s")
print("dose-response is linear down to 0.7 mg/L exposure levels.")
print("NTP explicitly stated insufficient data at this level.")
print("Confidence in this calculation: ~30%")
```

**Critical caveat:** This calculation extrapolates the Taylor et al. dose-response curve below the range where data is strongest (>1.5 mg/L). The NTP explicitly stated there is **insufficient data** to determine effects at 0.7 mg/L. This is an upper-bound risk estimate, not a prediction. Confidence: 30%.

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument 1: "The IQ studies are mostly from China with high fluoride levels — irrelevant to US CWF"
**Strength: Moderate.** True that 45 of 74 studies in Taylor et al. were from China, many at naturally high fluoride levels (>2 mg/L). However, 3 prospective cohort studies from Canada, Mexico, and Denmark — at exposure levels comparable to US CWF — also found inverse associations. The ELEMENT cohort (Mexico, Bashash et al. 2017) found effects at maternal urinary fluoride levels typical of US fluoridated communities. This counterargument weakens but does not eliminate the concern.

### Counterargument 2: "Topical programs can't replace CWF's equity reach"
**Strength: Strong.** This is the most legitimate pro-fluoridation argument. CWF requires zero individual compliance — everyone who drinks tap water is covered. School varnish programs miss preschoolers, homeschooled children, and children in schools with low participation. Toothpaste distribution requires behavioral change. A comprehensive targeted program must explicitly address these gaps or the equity argument stands.

### Counterargument 3: "The Cochrane review still shows SOME benefit — any caries reduction matters"
**Strength: Moderate.** True — the review found a possible 3 percentage-point increase in caries-free children even in the modern era. For a population of 11,000 children, that's ~330 children who might avoid at least one cavity. This is not zero. However, varnish programs achieve larger reductions with targeted delivery and no systemic exposure risk.

### Assumption Audit

| Assumption | Status | Risk if Wrong |
|------------|--------|---------------|
| Taylor et al. dose-response is approximately linear below 1.5 mg/L | **Uncertain** — NTP says insufficient data | IQ risk may be zero at 0.7 mg/L, eliminating primary concern |
| Cochrane 2024 contemporary effectiveness estimate is accurate | **Verified** — peer-reviewed systematic review | If CWF is more effective than Cochrane suggests, cost-benefit shifts |
| EPA rulemaking will restrict CWF | **Uncertain** — appeal pending, political dynamics | If EPA does not restrict, CWF remains legally defensible |
| Topical programs can achieve sufficient equity coverage | **Reasonable** — requires deliberate program design | If coverage gaps persist, disadvantaged children lose out |
| Fluoride exposure from other sources (toothpaste, diet) is already substantial | **Verified** — dental fluorosis data confirms widespread exposure | CWF adds to existing exposure, increasing marginal risk |

<details>
<summary>Failure Modes</summary>

1. **If you don't fluoridate and the IQ concern proves unfounded:** You spend more ($900K/yr vs $40K/yr) on targeted programs. Dental outcomes may be slightly worse for the hardest-to-reach populations. Reversible — you can fluoridate later.

2. **If you fluoridate and the IQ concern proves real at 0.7 mg/L:** Population-wide neurodevelopmental harm to children during the years of fluoridation. Not reversible for those cohorts. Legal liability exposure.

3. **If targeted programs have poor uptake:** Dental health disparities widen. This is the most likely failure mode of Option 1 and requires aggressive program design and monitoring.

**Asymmetry analysis:** The harm from unnecessary fluoridation (if IQ effects are real at 0.7 mg/L) is irreversible and population-wide. The harm from not fluoridating (slightly more cavities) is treatable and reversible. The precautionary principle favors Option 1.
</details>

## Recommendation

**Do not initiate community water fluoridation at this time.** Instead, invest in targeted topical fluoride programs (school varnish, toothpaste distribution, dental sealant partnerships) that deliver equal or superior dental protection without systemic exposure.

This recommendation holds under the following conditions:
- The Ninth Circuit has not yet resolved the *Food & Water Watch v. EPA* appeal
- EPA has not completed TSCA rulemaking on fluoride
- The NTP's "insufficient data at 0.7 mg/L" finding remains unresolved by new research

**Conditions that would change this recommendation:**
- Large, well-designed prospective cohort study finding no IQ effect at 0.7 mg/L -> reconsider CWF
- Ninth Circuit reverses district court and EPA declines rulemaking -> CWF becomes legally safer
- Targeted programs demonstrate poor equity coverage after 2 years -> CWF equity argument strengthens

**Confidence: 75%** — The precautionary principle and the weight of recent evidence favor waiting, but genuine uncertainty about effects at 0.7 mg/L means this is not an overwhelming case against fluoridation.
