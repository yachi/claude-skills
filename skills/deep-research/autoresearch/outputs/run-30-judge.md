### Fact-Check Details
- Claim 1: "TSMC 2025 revenue $122.54 billion" — Verified: YES — MacroTrends and TSMC investor relations report $122.27 billion for 2025; the $0.27B discrepancy (~0.2%) is likely due to the report using a TrendForce projection vs. final actuals, and is immaterial.
- Claim 2: "Taiwan has only 11 days of natural gas reserves" — Verified: YES — Multiple sources (S&P Global, Domino Theory, Focus Taiwan) confirm Taiwan maintains 8-11 days of LNG reserves, with a government target of 14 days by 2027, consistent with the report's claims.
- Claim 3: "CHIPS and Science Act: $52.7 billion for domestic semiconductor manufacturing" — Verified: YES — Wikipedia, McKinsey, Congress.gov CRS, and NIST all confirm the $52.7 billion appropriation figure for semiconductor manufacturing, R&D, and workforce development under P.L. 117-167.

### Critical Issues (if any)
- The Bloomberg Economics link cited for the "$5 trillion" blockade cost estimate uses a generic Bloomberg URL (`bloomberg.com/news/articles/`) rather than a specific article URL, making it unverifiable as cited. The claim itself is plausible but the sourcing is sloppy.
- The MIT Technology Review link for the "silicon shield" article uses a future date (2025/08/15) and a specific slug that may or may not resolve; this could not be independently verified via search.

### Missing Angles
- **Diplomatic/institutional mechanism**: The report recommends an "allied strategic semiconductor reserve" but doesn't address which allies, under what treaty framework, or how cost-sharing would work. A domain expert in international relations would flag this gap.
- **China's own stockpiling behavior**: China has been aggressively stockpiling semiconductor equipment and mature-node chips; the report doesn't analyze the adversary's parallel preparations.
- **Insurance/financial instruments**: Beyond physical stockpiling, financial hedging mechanisms (long-term supply agreements, options contracts on key chip categories) are not discussed as complementary tools.
- **Workforce continuity**: Even if fabs outside Taiwan ramp up, the specialized workforce knowledge concentrated in Taiwan is not easily replicated; this human capital dimension is underexplored.
