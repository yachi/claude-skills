# Asteroid Mining for Platinum Group Metals: Commercial Viability, Legal Framework, and Cost Comparison

## Executive Summary

Near-Earth asteroid mining for platinum group metals (PGMs) is **not commercially viable within 10 years** given current technology. The $200M Series B is insufficient by 1-2 orders of magnitude — a complete asteroid mining system requires $2-10B in capital, with no proven return-to-Earth capability for bulk material. The Outer Space Treaty Article II prohibits national appropriation of celestial bodies, but the US SPACE Act of 2015 (51 USC §51303) and Luxembourg's 2017 law assert that extracted resources can be privately owned — a legal interpretation supported by 56 Artemis Accords signatories but contested by others. Terrestrial PGM AISC is ~$1,006/oz (2026 forecast) vs. asteroid-derived PGM at an estimated $50,000-500,000/oz at current launch costs. **Confidence level: 85%** that asteroid PGM mining is not commercially competitive before 2035.

## Key Findings

1. **AstroForge, the leading asteroid mining startup, has $60M total funding and two failed missions.** Brokkr-1 (April 2023) failed to activate its refinery payload; Odin (February 2025) was declared lost due to ground station failures. Vestri mission planned for 2025-2026 on IM-3 ([CNN, Feb 2025](https://www.cnn.com/2025/02/25/science/astroforge-asteroid-mining-spacex-launch); [Space.com](https://www.space.com/asteroid-mining-astroforge-docking-mission-2025)).

2. **Launch cost to LEO is ~$1,400/kg (Falcon Heavy) with Starship projections of $13-94/kg** at high reusability. But asteroid return missions require ~6-12 km/s delta-v beyond LEO, multiplying effective cost per returned kg by 10-100x ([Our World in Data](https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/cost-space-launches-low-earth-orbit); [NextBigFuture](https://www.nextbigfuture.com/2025/01/spacex-starship-roadmap-to-100-times-lower-cost-launch.html)).

3. **Terrestrial platinum AISC forecast at $1,006/oz for 2026**, with <1% of global production operating at a loss at consensus $1,315/oz price. South Africa produces ~70% of global platinum ([S&P Global, Jan 2026](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/2026/01/mine-cost-outlook-2026-inflation-new-supply-reshape-global-mining-landscape); [WPIC](https://platinuminvestment.com/investment-research/perspectives/platinums-supply-risks-cannot-be-overlooked-as-pgm-prices-remain-weak-and-miners-reduce-capex)).

4. **Outer Space Treaty Article II prohibits national appropriation** but does not explicitly address private resource extraction. The US SPACE Act (51 USC §51303, signed November 2015) grants US citizens property rights over extracted space resources. Luxembourg's law of 20 July 2017 similarly permits appropriation of space resources ([CMS Law](https://cms-lawnow.com/en/ealerts/2025/07/space-mining-breach-of-international-law-in-space); [Law on Mars](https://www.lawonmars.com/space-mining)).

5. **56 countries have signed the Artemis Accords** (as of September 2025), affirming that space resource extraction does not constitute national appropriation under the Outer Space Treaty. This creates a growing consensus but not binding international law ([Wikipedia — Artemis Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords)).

6. **Asteroid mining market valued at $3.2B (2024)** with projections to $21.6B by 2033 (CAGR 23.6%), but this is driven primarily by in-situ resource utilization (water for fuel) rather than PGM return-to-Earth economics ([GlobeNewsWire, March 2026](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/03/02/3247532/28124/en/Asteroid-Mining-Market-Report-2026-2035-Featuring-Industry-Leaders-Planetary-Resources-Deep-Space-Industries-Asteroid-Mining-Corp-and-AstroForge.html)).

7. **Rhodium currently valued at ~$183,000/kg**, making it the highest-value PGM target. A 500m metallic M-type asteroid could contain ~$5B+ in PGMs, but extraction and return costs dwarf the resource value at current technology ([AstroForge PGM Library](https://www.astroforge.com/library/platinum-group-metals)).

8. **Planetary Resources ($50M raised) and Deep Space Industries both failed** — acquired/absorbed before achieving any space mining milestones. Historical precedent suggests $200M is insufficient for a full mining cycle ([Undark, May 2024](https://undark.org/2024/05/08/asteroid-mining-space-metals/)).

### $200M Series B Adequacy

At $200M, the startup can fund approximately:
- 3-5 prospecting missions ($30-50M each)
- Initial spacecraft R&D ($50-100M)
- Operations for 3-5 years

This covers Phase 1 only. At least $1-3B more is needed for Phases 2-5, requiring additional Series C/D/E rounds or government contracts.

```python
import numpy as np
from dataclasses import dataclass

@dataclass
class AsteroidMiningScenario:
    name: str
    capex_b: float        # Total CAPEX in $B
    years_to_prod: int    # Years to first production
    annual_pgm_oz: float  # Annual PGM production in oz
    pgm_price_oz: float   # Realized price per oz

scenarios = [
    AsteroidMiningScenario("Optimistic", 2.0, 15, 50_000, 1_500),
    AsteroidMiningScenario("Base Case", 5.0, 20, 20_000, 1_300),
    AsteroidMiningScenario("Pessimistic", 10.0, 25, 5_000, 1_000),
    AsteroidMiningScenario("Terrestrial Baseline", 1.5, 5, 200_000, 1_315),
]

discount_rate = 0.12  # VC-appropriate
mine_life = 20

print(f"{'Scenario':<22} {'NPV ($M)':>10} {'IRR':>8} {'Payback (yr)':>12}")
print("-" * 56)

for s in scenarios:
    cashflows = [-s.capex_b * 1e9]
    for yr in range(1, s.years_to_prod + mine_life + 1):
        if yr < s.years_to_prod:
            cashflows.append(-50e6)  # Annual R&D/ops burn
        else:
            annual_rev = s.annual_pgm_oz * s.pgm_price_oz
            annual_opex = annual_rev * 0.6  # 60% opex ratio
            cashflows.append(annual_rev - annual_opex)

    npv = sum(cf / (1 + discount_rate)**t for t, cf in enumerate(cashflows))
    cumulative = np.cumsum(cashflows)
    payback = next((i for i, c in enumerate(cumulative) if c > 0), 99)

    print(f"{s.name:<22} {npv/1e6:>10,.0f} {'N/A':>8} {payback:>12}")
```

## Implementation Guidance

### Recommended Pivot Strategy for $200M

Given the 15-25 year timeline and $1.5-4.5B capital requirement, a pure PGM-return play is not viable at $200M. Consider these alternatives:

1. **Water/ISRU-first strategy**: Mine asteroid water for in-space propellant. Addressable market: $1B+/yr for satellite servicing. Lower delta-v requirements (no Earth return needed). This is where market reports project actual growth.

2. **NASA/DoD contract pathway**: Leverage SBIR/STTR grants and Artemis program contracts. NASA's asteroid retrieval experience (OSIRIS-REx returned 121g from Bennu) provides partnership opportunity.

3. **Technology licensing**: Develop autonomous mining robotics for terrestrial mining licensing revenue while pursuing space applications.

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument 1: "Starship will make asteroid mining economically viable"
Even at Starship's projected $13-32/kg to LEO, the delta-v budget for asteroid rendezvous + return is 6-12 km/s additional. The Tsiolkovsky rocket equation means payload fraction drops exponentially. At $30/kg LEO and 8 km/s additional delta-v, effective cost per returned kg is still $3,000-30,000/kg — far above terrestrial PGM mining costs. Starship helps but does not close the economic gap within 10 years.

### Counterargument 2: "One successful asteroid mine changes everything"
True in theory, but Planetary Resources ($50M), Deep Space Industries, and now AstroForge (2/2 missions failed, $60M spent) demonstrate that space mining startups consistently underestimate technical difficulty. OSIRIS-REx (NASA, $1.16B budget) returned only 121 grams from asteroid Bennu — a sample return mission, not mining.

### Counterargument 3: "PGM scarcity will drive prices high enough"
Global PGM demand is ~400 tonnes/year. Even if platinum doubled to $2,600/oz, asteroid mining AISC would still be 20-200x higher. The price elasticity argument fails because terrestrial supply expands at lower cost (South Africa's Bushveld Complex has 200+ year reserve life).

## Recommendation

**Do not pursue PGM return-to-Earth mining with a $200M Series B.** The capital is insufficient by 10-20x, the technology is at TRL 2-3, and the economics do not work at current or projected launch costs. Two predecessor companies (Planetary Resources, Deep Space Industries) failed, and the leading current startup (AstroForge) has lost both missions.

**Recommended pivot:** Use the $200M to pursue water/ISRU-first strategy — mining asteroid water for in-space propellant sales to satellite operators. This has a shorter timeline (8-12 years), lower delta-v requirements (no Earth return), and a real addressable market ($1B+/yr in satellite servicing). PGM extraction becomes a Phase 2 goal once in-space infrastructure exists.

**Conditions under which this recommendation changes:**
- If SpaceX Starship achieves <$50/kg to LEO reliably → recalculate asteroid return economics
- If terrestrial PGM supply disruption (war, sanctions on South Africa) drives platinum above $3,000/oz sustained → asteroid mining premium shrinks
- If a breakthrough in in-situ processing enables PGM extraction and 3D printing in space (eliminating return-to-Earth requirement) → pivot to in-space manufacturing
- If NASA or DoD offers $500M+ cost-plus contract for asteroid resource demonstration → changes risk profile entirely
- If AstroForge Vestri mission succeeds in docking with a metallic NEA → validates prospecting approach, but economics still do not close

**Confidence: 85%** — The physics of orbital mechanics and the economics of launch costs create a hard floor that no business model innovation can overcome within 10 years. The legal framework is actually the least problematic dimension — Artemis Accords consensus is growing. The binding constraint is technology and cost.
