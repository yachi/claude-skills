# Can a Municipality Convert a Shuttered 500 MWe BWR Nuclear Plant Site Into a Data Center Campus for $50M in 12 Months?

## Executive Summary

**No. The premise is fundamentally flawed on multiple dimensions.** A "safely defueled" reactor does not mean a decommissioned or releasable site. The municipality has likely acquired an active NRC license obligation, not a shovel-ready property. Realistic timeline: **7-15 years** before unrestricted site release. Realistic total cost: **$500M-$1.1B** for decommissioning alone, plus **$50-120M** for the data center itself. The $50M budget covers roughly **5-10%** of the actual decommissioning cost and the 12-month timeline is off by an order of magnitude. **Confidence: 92%** — this is well-established regulatory territory with extensive precedent.

**Critical immediate action:** Engage nuclear regulatory counsel to assess the NRC license status and decommissioning trust fund obligations before any construction planning.

## Premise Challenge

The question contains three flawed assumptions that must be addressed before any useful planning can occur:

### Flaw 1: "Safely defueled" ≠ "Decommissioned"

A reactor that has been defueled — meaning spent fuel has been removed from the reactor vessel and placed in a spent fuel pool or dry cask storage — is still under active NRC regulation. Under **10 CFR 50.82**, the licensee must submit a Post-Shutdown Decommissioning Activities Report (PSDAR) and eventually a License Termination Plan (LTP) at least 2 years before planned license termination ([NRC 10 CFR 50.82](https://www.nrc.gov/reading-rm/doc-collections/cfr/part050/part050-0082)). The site cannot be released for unrestricted use until it meets the radiological criteria in **10 CFR 20.1402**: residual radioactivity must not exceed **25 mrem (0.25 mSv) per year** TEDE to the critical group, reduced to ALARA ([NRC 10 CFR 20.1402](https://www.nrc.gov/reading-rm/doc-collections/cfr/part020/part020-1402)).

**Precedent:** Vermont Yankee (similar BWR, closed 2014) will not complete decommissioning until ~2026-2030 at earliest. Connecticut Yankee completed decommissioning in 2007 but the site is still not fully redeveloped. Neither site has been converted to a data center ([Vermont Yankee Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vermont_Yankee_Nuclear_Power_Plant); [Connecticut Yankee NDC](https://decommissioningcollaborative.org/connecticut-yankee/)).

### Flaw 2: "$1 purchase price" likely means transferred liabilities

A nuclear plant site sold for $1 is a red flag, not a bargain. The seller is likely transferring the decommissioning liability along with the property. Under **10 CFR 50.75**, licensees must maintain a decommissioning trust fund. If the trust fund is underfunded (common for 1970s-era plants), the new owner may inherit a multi-hundred-million-dollar liability.

**Precedent:** Holtec International acquired Indian Point for decommissioning — the plant came with a $2.3B decommissioning fund, not a $1 price tag. The fund, not the purchase price, is what matters ([EIA](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=33792)).

### Flaw 3: $50M budget is 5-10% of actual cost

NRC's minimum formula-based decommissioning estimate for a single reactor unit is ~$559M. Site-specific analyses for BWRs of this vintage run $544M-$1.1B. For a 500 MWe unit, using the per-MW cost range of $1.07-$1.22M/MWe (sub-1100 MWe plants), the decommissioning cost alone is **$535M-$610M** ([World Nuclear Association](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/nuclear-fuel-cycle/nuclear-waste/decommissioning-nuclear-facilities); [ANS — NRC Cost Guidance 2025](https://www.ans.org/news/2025-02-19/article-6772/nrc-report-updates-decommissioning-cost-guidance/)).

The data center construction on top of this would cost an additional **$50-120M** depending on capacity (5-10 MW critical load at $10-12M/MW) ([Cushman & Wakefield 2025](https://cushwake.cld.bz/Data-Center-Development-Cost-Guide-2025)).

## Key Findings

1. **Decommissioning a 500 MWe BWR costs $535M-$1.1B** (NRC minimum formula: $559M; site-specific estimates typically higher). The $50M budget is insufficient by at least 10x for decommissioning alone ([NRC Backgrounder](https://www.nrc.gov/reading-rm/doc-collections/fact-sheets/decommissioning); [World Nuclear Association](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/nuclear-fuel-cycle/nuclear-waste/decommissioning-nuclear-facilities)).

2. **DECON (immediate dismantling) takes minimum 7 years; SAFSTOR can extend to 60 years.** The NRC allows up to 60 years for decommissioning completion under 10 CFR 50.82. Kewaunee estimates completion in 2073 (60+ years from closure) ([NRC](https://www.nrc.gov/waste/decommissioning/process); [EIA](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=33792)).

3. **Unrestricted site release requires ≤25 mrem/year residual dose** per 10 CFR 20.1402. A 1970s BWR likely has activated structural components, contaminated concrete, and potential soil contamination requiring extensive characterization and remediation ([NRC 10 CFR 20.1402](https://www.nrc.gov/reading-rm/doc-collections/cfr/part020/part020-1402)).

4. **Partial site release is possible** — the NRC allows releasing portions of a reactor site for unrestricted use before the LTP is approved (Federal Register, 66 FR 46230). This could allow data center construction on non-contaminated portions while decommissioning continues on the reactor island ([Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2001/09/04/01-22139/releasing-part-of-a-power-reactor-site-or-facility-for-unrestricted-use-before-the-nrc-approves-the)).

5. **Nuclear sites DO have advantages for data centers**: existing grid interconnection (500 MW capacity), cooling water infrastructure, large land parcels, and security infrastructure. Vermont Yankee is being considered for battery energy storage ([Vermont Public](https://www.vermontpublic.org/local-news/2025-10-24/texas-company-former-yankee-site-battery-energy-storage-system)).

6. **Data center construction costs $10-12M per MW** of critical load (2025 average), with AI-optimized facilities reaching $20M+/MW. A 10 MW campus would cost $100-120M for construction alone, excluding land prep ([Cushman & Wakefield 2025](https://cushwake.cld.bz/Data-Center-Development-Cost-Guide-2025); [JLL 2026 Outlook](https://www.jll.com/en-us/insights/market-outlook/data-center-outlook)).

7. **Spent fuel remains on-site indefinitely** in dry cask storage (ISFSI) even after decommissioning. This area remains under NRC license and cannot be used for the data center. Connecticut Yankee's ISFSI is still operational decades after plant closure ([Connecticut Yankee NDC](https://decommissioningcollaborative.org/connecticut-yankee/)).

8. **NRC updated decommissioning cost formula in 2025** (NUREG-1307 Revision 20), with LLW burial cost factors declining slightly — but this does not materially change the order-of-magnitude gap ([ANS 2025](https://www.ans.org/news/2025-02-19/article-6772/nrc-report-updates-decommissioning-cost-guidance/)).

## Implementation Guidance

### What the Municipality Should Actually Do

Given the massive gap between expectations and reality, here is a realistic action plan:

#### Immediate (Month 1-3): Stop and Assess

1. **Hire nuclear regulatory counsel** — a firm specializing in NRC decommissioning (e.g., Morgan Lewis, Pillsbury Winthrop, Hogan Lovells nuclear practice). Budget: $200K-$500K for initial assessment.

2. **Determine NRC license status**: Is the license still active? Has a PSDAR been filed? Is the decommissioning trust fund funded? Was the license properly transferred in the sale?

```bash

# NRC maintains a public database of all decommissioning reactors

```

3. **Commission a radiological site characterization** — before any planning, you need to know the extent of contamination. This alone costs $1-5M for a 500 MWe BWR site.

#### Short-Term (Month 3-12): Explore Viable Paths

4. **Evaluate partial site release**: Petition NRC under 66 FR 46230 to release non-contaminated portions of the site (parking areas, administrative buildings, switchyard area) for data center use while decommissioning continues on the reactor island.

5. **Assess the grid interconnection value**: The existing 500 MW grid connection is extremely valuable. New utility-scale interconnections take 3-5 years and cost millions. This asset alone could justify the site — if the decommissioning timeline is manageable.

6. **Explore DOE/NRC decommissioning partnerships**: Engage with entities like Holtec International or EnergySolutions that specialize in decommissioning and could partner on the project, bringing both expertise and funding.

#### Long-Term (Year 1-5): If Proceeding

7. **Secure financing**: Municipal bonds, DOE Loan Programs Office, private data center developer partnership. A hyperscaler (AWS, Google, Microsoft) may be interested in the grid connection and could fund decommissioning as part of a long-term site lease.

8. **Develop phased construction plan**: Build data center on released portions while decommissioning progresses. This is the only path that leverages the site's advantages while managing the timeline.

## Adversarial Review

### Counterargument 1: "Partial site release could allow construction within 2-3 years"

**Evidence for**: The NRC has approved partial site releases at multiple facilities. If the switchyard area, parking lots, and administrative areas were never within the radiological controlled area, they could potentially be released with a relatively straightforward characterization survey. Vermont Yankee's town is already planning site redevelopment on released portions ([Vernon VT Redevelopment](https://vernonvt.org/vermont-yankee-site-redevelopment/)).

**Rebuttal**: This is the most viable path, and the analysis recommends it. However, even partial release requires NRC petition, radiological characterization of the release area, public comment period, and NRC staff review — a process that typically takes 18-36 months, not 12. The $50M budget is still insufficient for the overall project but could cover the initial data center construction IF decommissioning is funded separately. The counterargument is partially correct but doesn't fix the timeline or budget gap.

### Counterargument 2: "Hyperscalers are desperate for sites with grid connections — they'll fund everything"

**Evidence for**: Data center land costs have risen 23% year-over-year. Grid interconnection queues are 3-5+ years long. A 500 MW grid connection is enormously valuable in today's market. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon have all expressed interest in nuclear-adjacent or nuclear-powered data centers.

**Rebuttal**: This is a plausible financing strategy and should be explored. A hyperscaler might fund decommissioning ($500M-$1B) in exchange for a long-term site lease with guaranteed grid capacity. However, this fundamentally changes the project from "municipality builds data center" to "municipality leases site to hyperscaler who manages decommissioning and builds their own facility." The municipality's role shifts from developer to landlord, and the 12-month timeline is still unrealistic. The $50M budget becomes irrelevant if a hyperscaler is funding the project, but the municipality loses operational control.

### Counterargument 3: "The seller said it was 'safely defueled' — surely the hard work is done"

**Evidence for**: Defueling is a significant milestone. Once spent fuel is in dry cask storage, the major radiological hazard is contained and the site's risk profile decreases substantially.

**Rebuttal**: Defueling addresses maybe 5% of the decommissioning scope. The remaining work includes: reactor pressure vessel segmentation and disposal, steam generator removal (BWR: steam separators/dryers), decontamination of primary coolant systems, removal of activated biological shield concrete, soil remediation, building demolition, Final Status Survey across the entire site, and NRC review/approval of the LTP. "Safely defueled" is roughly equivalent to "we moved the furniture out of a house that needs to be demolished, decontaminated, and rebuilt." The characterization that the "hard work is done" fundamentally misunderstands nuclear decommissioning scope.

### Assumption Audit

| Assumption | Status | Risk if Wrong |

| Site has not yet been released for unrestricted use | Very likely (NRC database verifiable) | If already released, project becomes viable — but this is extremely unlikely for a $1 sale |

| Grid interconnection is still active and maintained | Must verify with utility | If disconnected, reconnection cost/timeline negates a key advantage |
| NRC will approve partial site release | Precedented but not guaranteed | If denied, no data center activity until full decom complete (7-15 years) |

## Recommendation

**Do not proceed with the current plan.** The $50M budget and 12-month timeline are not feasible — they underestimate costs by 10-20x and timeline by 7-15x. Instead:

1. **Immediately** ($50K): Engage NRC counsel to assess license status and liabilities
2. **Month 1-6** ($500K-$1M): Radiological site characterization + trust fund assessment
3. **Month 6-12**: Explore hyperscaler partnership for grid connection value — this is the realistic financing path
4. **Year 1-3**: Petition NRC for partial site release; begin data center construction on released areas if approved
5. **Year 3-15**: Continue decommissioning in partnership with specialized decom company

**Confidence: 92%** — The regulatory framework (10 CFR 50.82, 10 CFR 20.1402) is unambiguous. Decommissioning cost ranges are well-established across dozens of US plants. The only meaningful uncertainty is whether partial site release would be approved and on what timeline.

**This recommendation changes if:**
- The site has ALREADY been fully decommissioned and received NRC unrestricted release — verify immediately (would make the $50M budget and ~24-month timeline realistic for data center construction alone)
- A hyperscaler commits to funding decommissioning — transforms the economics entirely
- NRC grants expedited partial site release — shortens timeline to 3-5 years but does not reduce total decommissioning cost
