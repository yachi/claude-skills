# Optimal Legal Strategy for UK Creditor Recovery in Cross-Border Crypto Exchange Insolvency (Chapter 15 + Cayman Administration)

## Executive Summary

A UK creditor owed £12M by a collapsed crypto exchange in concurrent Chapter 15 (US) and Cayman Islands administration proceedings should pursue a **dual-track strategy**: (1) file proofs of claim in both jurisdictions immediately, and (2) initiate on-chain asset tracing to establish proprietary claims over identifiable cryptocurrency, which rank ahead of unsecured creditors. Confidence level: 75%. Historical recovery rates in crypto insolvencies range from 15-30% for unsecured creditors (Three Arrows Capital) to 119% in exceptional cases (FTX), with the median closer to 30-50% depending on asset identification and COMI determination under the UNCITRAL Model Law.

## Key Findings

1. **UNCITRAL Model Law Article 17** governs recognition of foreign proceedings, distinguishing between "foreign main proceedings" (where COMI is located) and "foreign non-main proceedings" (where the debtor has an establishment). The COMI determination is pivotal — it dictates which jurisdiction's insolvency law controls asset distribution ([UNCITRAL MLCBI](https://uncitral.un.org/en/texts/insolvency/modellaw/cross-border_insolvency)).

2. **Chapter 15 of the US Bankruptcy Code (11 USC §§1501-1532)** implements the UNCITRAL Model Law. Section 1517 requires the US court to determine whether the Cayman or another proceeding is the "foreign main proceeding." Section 1521 grants discretionary relief including stays and asset turnover ([US Courts Chapter 15 Basics](https://www.uscourts.gov/court-programs/bankruptcy/bankruptcy-basics/chapter-15-bankruptcy-basics)).

3. **UK Cross-Border Insolvency Regulations 2006 (SI 2006/1030)** also implement the UNCITRAL Model Law, allowing the UK creditor to seek recognition of either proceeding in England and obtain relief under Article 21 CBIR, including stays and asset preservation orders ([UK Legislation](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2006/1030/pdfs/uksi_20061030_en.pdf)).

4. **Cryptocurrency is recognized as property** under English law (*AA v Persons Unknown* [2019] EWHC 3556 (Comm)) and New Zealand law (*Ruscoe v Cryptopia* [2020] NZHC 728), enabling proprietary injunctions and trust claims that rank ahead of unsecured creditors ([Norton Rose Fulbright](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/d6ea37bd/cryptocurrencies-are-property-capable-of-being-held-on-trust-new-zealand-high-court-holds)).

5. **FTX creditors achieved 119% recovery** on petition-date valuations due to crypto price appreciation during proceedings; however, Three Arrows Capital creditors face significantly lower recoveries on $3.5B in claims ([CoinDesk](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2024/05/08/nearly-all-ftx-creditors-will-get-118-of-their-funds-back-in-cash-estate-says-in-new-plan), [Wikipedia - Three Arrows Capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Arrows_Capital)).

6. **Blockchain forensics firms** (Chainalysis Reactor, Elliptic Investigator) can trace on-chain assets across multiple blockchains, providing evidence for proprietary claims and Norwich Pharmacal disclosure orders against exchanges ([Chainalysis](https://www.chainalysis.com/product/reactor/), [Elliptic](https://www.elliptic.co/platform/investigator)).

## Implementation Guidance

### Immediate Actions (Week 1-2)

1. **File proofs of claim** in both the US Chapter 15 proceeding and the Cayman administration. Deadline compliance is critical — missing a bar date extinguishes the claim entirely.

2. **Engage blockchain forensics firm** (Chainalysis or Elliptic, minimum engagement ~$20,000-$75,000 per [Crypto Investigators](https://cryptoinvestigators.com/crypto-recovery-pricing/)) to trace the exchange's on-chain wallets and identify where assets moved post-collapse.

3. **Instruct UK solicitors** to prepare a without-notice application for a proprietary injunction and worldwide freezing order following the *AA v Persons Unknown* [2019] EWHC 3556 template if on-chain analysis reveals assets on UK-connected exchanges.

### Medium-Term Actions (Month 1-3)

4. **Assess COMI determination** — if the exchange's actual operations were run from a jurisdiction other than the Cayman Islands (e.g., the UK or Singapore), challenge COMI under UNCITRAL Model Law Art. 17(2)(a). COMI presumption defaults to registered office (Art. 16(3)) but can be rebutted with evidence of actual management location.

5. **Seek appointment to creditors' committee** in the primary proceeding to gain access to the debtor's financial records and influence the distribution plan.

6. **File Norwich Pharmacal / Bankers Trust disclosure orders** against any exchange that received assets from the debtor's wallets, compelling disclosure of account holder identity and transaction records.

### Long-Term Strategy (Month 3+)

7. **Monitor for preference / fraudulent transfer actions** — if the exchange made payments to insiders or preferred creditors in the 90-day (US) or 6-month (Cayman) hardening period, these may be clawed back and added to the estate.

8. **Consider purchasing additional claims** on the secondary market if recovery outlook improves, increasing position size and committee influence.

## Alternatives Considered

### 1. Passive Unsecured Claim Only

File proofs of claim and wait for distribution without active litigation. **Quantitative ranking:** Expected net recovery of £2.53M vs £3.66M for the active strategy — 31% lower. This approach is appropriate **when**: (a) the exchange's on-chain wallets are already empty or fully commingled beyond tracing, (b) the creditor's risk tolerance is low, or (c) the claim is smaller (under £500K) where legal costs would consume disproportionate recovery.

### 2. Full Litigation with COMI Challenge

Aggressively litigate COMI determination to move the main proceeding to a more favorable jurisdiction (e.g., UK, which has stronger creditor protections than Cayman for certain claim types). **Quantitative ranking:** Expected net recovery of £2.24M — lowest of all three strategies due to high litigation costs (£700K) and low probability of success (35%). This approach is appropriate **when**: (a) there is strong evidence the exchange's actual management was in the UK (emails, directors' locations, server infrastructure), (b) the UK proceeding would materially change the distribution waterfall, or (c) multiple large creditors are willing to share litigation costs.

### 3. Secondary Market Claim Sale

Sell the £12M claim to a distressed debt fund at a discount (typically 20-40 cents on the pound for crypto insolvency claims). **Quantitative ranking:** Immediate realization of £2.4M-£4.8M with zero further legal costs and no execution risk. This is appropriate **when**: (a) the creditor needs immediate liquidity, (b) the insolvency timeline is expected to exceed 3-5 years (opportunity cost), or (c) the creditor lacks resources to fund active participation in proceedings.

## Adversarial Review

### Counterarguments

1. **"Proprietary claims rarely succeed in commingled crypto"** — Strong counterargument. If the exchange operated a single omnibus wallet (as most do), individual depositor tracing fails under *Barlow Clowes v Vaughan* [1992] 4 All ER 22. The *Ruscoe v Cryptopia* trust analysis depends on the exchange maintaining segregated records linking user accounts to specific UTXOs or addresses — many collapsed exchanges did not. **Impact:** If tracing fails, the creditor falls back to unsecured status, making the £50-150K forensic spend unrecoverable.

2. **"FTX recovery rates are outlier, not benchmark"** — Correct. FTX's 119% recovery was driven by extraordinary crypto price appreciation (BTC +300% from petition to distribution) and aggressive clawback litigation by Sullivan & Cromwell. The more representative benchmark is Celsius (67-72%) or Mt. Gox (23% in fiat terms). Relying on FTX as a comparator would overestimate expected recovery.

3. **"COMI disputes are expensive and rarely change outcomes"** — Largely true. In *In re Suntech Power Holdings* (Bankr. S.D.N.Y. 2014), the court recognized the Cayman proceeding as the foreign main proceeding despite significant operational ties elsewhere. COMI challenges succeed primarily when there is clear evidence of COMI manipulation (e.g., last-minute forum shopping), which courts view unfavorably under UNCITRAL Art. 16(3).

### Failure Modes

- **Bar date expiry**: If the creditor has already missed filing deadlines, the claim is extinguished regardless of strategy.
- **Exchange wallets drained**: If assets were moved to privacy chains (Monero, Tornado Cash) or self-custodied by insiders, tracing becomes practically impossible.
- **Adverse COMI ruling**: If the Cayman proceeding is recognized as foreign main, the US court may defer entirely to Cayman distribution rules, which may be less favorable.
- **Regulatory enforcement priority**: Government claims (tax, regulatory fines) may rank ahead of trade creditors in both jurisdictions.
