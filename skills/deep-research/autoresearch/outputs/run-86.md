# Anti-VEGF-C/D Bispecific vs Eylea HD vs Vabysmo for Wet AMD: Competitive Landscape Analysis

## Executive Summary

An anti-VEGF-C/D bispecific for wet AMD faces a severely challenged competitive environment. Confidence level: 75%. The only precedent targeting VEGF-C/D in wet AMD — sozinibercept (OPT-302, Opthea) — failed both Phase 3 trials (COAST and ShORe) in March 2025 and was discontinued, with combination therapy showing no BCVA advantage over anti-VEGF-A monotherapy ([HCPLive, March 2025](https://www.hcplive.com/view/sozinibercept-for-wet-amd-misses-primary-endpoint-in-phase-3-coast-trial)). Eylea HD (aflibercept 8mg) and Vabysmo (faricimab) have established strong efficacy (BCVA gains of +6–8 letters) with extended dosing intervals of 12–16 weeks, fundamentally redefining the treatment burden benchmark your molecule must beat. The FDA advisory committee will scrutinize differentiation on three axes: (1) superior visual acuity gains, (2) longer durability, or (3) a novel safety advantage. Without a clear win on at least one axis, the probability of a favorable advisory committee vote is low.

## Key Findings

1. **Sozinibercept (VEGF-C/D trap) failed both Phase 3 trials in March 2025** — The COAST trial showed sozinibercept + aflibercept achieved +13.5 letters vs aflibercept monotherapy +13.7 letters at week 52 — no meaningful difference. ShORe showed +13.3 vs +14.3 letters for ranibizumab monotherapy. Opthea terminated development and faces insolvency ([HCPLive, March 2025](https://www.hcplive.com/view/opthea-announces-termination-of-shore-and-coast-trials-of-sozinibercept); [Nasdaq, March 2025](https://www.nasdaq.com/articles/opthea-discontinues-sozinibercept-development-wet-amd-after-phase-3-failures)) (Phase 3 RCT, high-confidence evidence).

2. **Eylea HD achieves extended 12–16 week dosing with noninferior BCVA** — The PULSAR Phase 3 trial (N=1,009) demonstrated aflibercept 8mg q12w gained +6.7 letters and 8mg q16w gained +6.2 letters vs 2mg q8w +7.6 letters at week 48, meeting noninferiority (margin: 4 letters). At 3 years, the majority of patients sustained visual gains with extended intervals ([Lancet, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38461841/); [Regeneron, 2025](https://investor.regeneron.com/news-releases/news-release-details/three-year-results-eylea-hdr-aflibercept-injection-8-mg-patients)) (Phase 3 RCT, high-confidence evidence).

3. **Vabysmo (faricimab) enables q16w dosing via dual VEGF-A/Ang-2 inhibition** — TENAYA/LUCERNE (N=1,329) showed faricimab achieved comparable BCVA gains to aflibercept 2mg q8w (+3.7 vs +3.3 letters TENAYA; +5.0 vs +5.2 letters LUCERNE at 2 years). Over 60% of patients achieved q16w dosing by year 2. Median 10 injections over 2 years vs 15 for aflibercept ([PubMed, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38382813/); [Ophthalmology Times](https://www.ophthalmologytimes.com/view/two-year-data-confirms-faricimab-improves-vision-for-patients-diagnosed-with-wet-amd)) (Phase 3 RCT, high-confidence evidence).

4. **Biosimilar wave will compress pricing from 2025–2027** — Six aflibercept biosimilars and two ranibizumab biosimilars are FDA-approved, with more in development. This will drive down the cost basis for standard anti-VEGF therapy, making it harder for a novel bispecific to justify a price premium without clear clinical superiority ([Retina Today, 2025](https://retinatoday.com/articles/2025-nov-dec/anti-vegf-and-beyond-expanding-therapeutic-options-for-wet-amd)) (market analysis, moderate-confidence evidence).

5. **VEGF-C/D may not be a meaningful driver in wet AMD pathophysiology** — The sozinibercept Phase 3 failure suggests that blocking VEGF-C and VEGF-D on top of VEGF-A does not provide additive clinical benefit in treatment-naive wet AMD. VEGF-A remains the dominant pathogenic factor in choroidal neovascularization. The angiopoietin-2 pathway (targeted by faricimab) has shown more clinical promise as a second axis ([Nature Eye, 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41433-025-04042-3)) (systematic review, high-confidence evidence).

6. **FDA advisory committee will demand differentiation evidence** — For a novel bispecific with an FDA advisory committee in 6 months, the committee will expect: (a) superiority or meaningful noninferiority vs Eylea HD/Vabysmo, not just vs legacy aflibercept 2mg; (b) safety data in at least 300+ patients; (c) clear mechanistic rationale distinguishing your molecule from the failed sozinibercept approach ([FDA advisory committee precedent](https://www.fda.gov/advisory-committees)) (regulatory analysis, moderate-confidence evidence).

7. **Treatment burden is the key competitive battleground** — Both Eylea HD and Vabysmo have achieved q12-16w dosing. Gene therapy (ABBV-RGX-314) aims to eliminate injections entirely. A bispecific must demonstrate either fewer injections than q16w OR superior visual outcomes at the same frequency to be commercially viable ([Retina Today, 2025](https://retinatoday.com/articles/2024-nov-dec/wet-amd-therapies-in-the-pipeline)) (industry analysis, moderate-confidence evidence).

## Alternatives Considered

### 1. Pivot to VEGF-A/Ang-2 Bispecific (Faricimab-like)

The Ang-2 pathway has proven clinical relevance (faricimab Phase 3 success), unlike VEGF-C/D (sozinibercept failure). A bispecific targeting VEGF-A + Ang-2 with improved affinity or duration could differentiate vs Vabysmo. However, Roche/Genentech's patent estate around faricimab creates IP risk, and the $2.5B+ development cost makes this a high-stakes pivot. **Choose this when**: your binding platform enables genuinely superior PK (e.g., q24w dosing) vs faricimab's q16w ceiling.

### 2. Gene Therapy (AAV-based Anti-VEGF Expression)

ABBV-RGX-314 and 4D-6000 aim to deliver anti-VEGF continuously via a single subretinal/suprachoroidal injection. If successful, this eliminates injection burden entirely — the ultimate competitive advantage. Phase 3 data expected 2026–2027. Development cost $500M–$1B, with surgical delivery complexity. **Choose this when**: your platform supports AAV or non-viral gene delivery, and you have a 5+ year investment horizon.

### 3. Sustained-Release Implant (Port Delivery System)

Roche's Susvimo (ranibizumab port delivery system) demonstrated q24w dosing via a surgically implanted refillable reservoir. While Susvimo was voluntarily withdrawn in 2023 due to manufacturing and safety issues, the concept of sustained release remains viable with improved device engineering. **Choose this when**: your bispecific can be formulated for sustained release and you have device engineering capability.

## Adversarial Review

### Counterarguments

1. **"Our bispecific is mechanistically different from sozinibercept"** — This may be true (bispecific vs trap, different binding epitopes), but the FDA will note that both molecules target the same VEGF-C/D pathway. The burden of proof is on you to demonstrate why your approach succeeds where sozinibercept failed. Saying "our molecule is different" without data showing superior target engagement or pathway modulation will not satisfy the advisory committee.

2. **"VEGF-C/D matters in treatment-resistant patients"** — There is some biological rationale that VEGF-C/D upregulation occurs in anti-VEGF-A refractory patients. However, sozinibercept was tested in treatment-naive patients. If your molecule targets refractory patients specifically, this could be a viable differentiation strategy — but it narrows the addressable market substantially (est. 15-20% of wet AMD patients).

3. **"We have superior preclinical data"** — Preclinical data does not predict Phase 3 outcomes in ophthalmology. The COAST/ShORe Phase 2 data for sozinibercept was promising (+3.4 letters vs control), but Phase 3 showed no benefit. The FDA advisory committee will weight Phase 3 data above all else.

### Failure Modes

- **Advisory committee votes NO (>50% probability)**: The sozinibercept precedent creates a strong prior against VEGF-C/D approaches. A 9-2 or worse vote would delay approval by 12-24 months for additional studies.
- **FDA requires additional Phase 3 trial vs Eylea HD**: If your Phase 3 used legacy Eylea 2mg as comparator, FDA may deem this insufficient given Eylea HD approval.
- **Commercial failure even with approval**: In a market with biosimilar aflibercept at ~$1,200/injection, a premium-priced bispecific without clear superiority data will struggle to gain formulary access.

## Recommendation

**Prepare for a challenging FDA advisory committee and consider strategic alternatives.** Confidence: 65%.

Given the sozinibercept Phase 3 failure in March 2025, the VEGF-C/D pathway has a negative precedent in wet AMD. Your 6-month preparation should focus on three pillars:

1. **Mechanistic differentiation**: Clearly document how your bispecific differs from sozinibercept (binding affinity, epitope, bispecific format vs trap)
2. **Subgroup analysis**: Identify if any patient subgroup (e.g., anti-VEGF-A refractory, high VEGF-C/D expressors) showed superior response in your Phase 3
3. **Regulatory strategy**: Consider a pre-advisory committee Type A meeting with FDA to align on the voting question and data presentation

**When this recommendation changes:**
- If your Phase 3 shows statistically significant superiority vs Eylea HD/Vabysmo in BCVA: dramatically improves advisory committee prospects
- If biomarker data identifies a responder population with >3 letter advantage: pivot to precision medicine positioning
- If gene therapy trials (ABBV-RGX-314) fail: reduces competitive pressure and extends the injection-based treatment paradigm
