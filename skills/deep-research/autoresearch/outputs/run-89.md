# Thorium MSR vs Gen III+ PWR for 1GW Baseload in a Country Without Nuclear Regulatory Framework

## Executive Summary

For a country with no existing nuclear regulatory framework seeking 1GW baseload, a **Gen III+ PWR (AP1000 or EPR) is the strongly recommended choice over a thorium MSR**. Confidence level: 88%. The thorium MSR is not commercially available — the most advanced demonstrator (China's TMSR-LF1) is a 2MWt prototype that only achieved thorium-uranium conversion in November 2025 ([CAS, 2025](https://english.cas.cn/newsroom/cas_media/202511/t20251104_1095922.shtml)). No thorium MSR has been licensed, built, or operated at commercial scale anywhere in the world. The Gen III+ PWR, by contrast, has four operational units (Vogtle 3&4, Sanmen 1&2, Haiyang 1&2), a proven licensing pathway under IAEA SSR-2/1, an established vendor ecosystem (Westinghouse, EDF, KHNP), and an NOAK overnight cost projected at $4,625–6,200/kW with LCOE of $50–80/MWh ([MIT CANES, 2024](https://canes.mit.edu/media/overnight-capital-cost-of-the-next-ap1000)). Building a nuclear regulatory framework from scratch under the IAEA Milestones Approach takes 10-15 years ([IAEA NG-G-3.1 Rev. 2, 2024](https://www.iaea.org/topics/infrastructure-development/milestones-approach)); adding the regulatory uncertainty of an unproven reactor type (MSR) would extend this by an additional 5-10+ years.

## Key Findings

1. **No thorium MSR exists at commercial scale — the most advanced is 2MWt** — China's TMSR-LF1 (Shanghai Institute of Applied Physics) is the only operating thorium MSR worldwide. It achieved full 2MWt power in June 2024, successfully bred U-233 from thorium in November 2025, and plans a 10MW demonstrator by 2029 and 100MW units by 2035. This is 1,000x below the requested 1GW scale ([World Nuclear News, 2025](https://www.world-nuclear-news.org/articles/chinese-msr-achieves-conversion-of-thorium-uranium-fuel); [Power Magazine, 2025](https://www.powermag.com/chinas-molten-salt-reactor-reaches-thorium-uranium-conversion-milestone/)) (primary source, verified).

2. **Gen III+ PWR overnight cost: $4,625–6,200/kW NOAK; LCOE $50–80/MWh** — MIT's 2024 study projects AP1000 NOAK overnight cost at $4,625/kW with unsubsidized LCOE of $66/MWh, or $50/MWh with 40% ITC. FOAK costs are significantly higher: Vogtle 3&4 came in at ~$13,500/kW ($35B for 2.2GW). The fourth two-unit plant is projected at $6,200/kW in a "moderate" learning scenario ([MIT CANES, 2024](https://web.mit.edu/kshirvan/www/research/ANP201%20TR%20CANES.pdf); [World Nuclear Association](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/economic-aspects/economics-of-nuclear-power)) (peer-reviewed analysis, high-confidence evidence).

3. **IAEA Milestones Approach: 10-15 years for a new nuclear country** — The IAEA's three-phase milestones approach (NG-G-3.1) guides countries from initial consideration to first NPP operation. Phase 1 (knowledge commitment): 2-3 years. Phase 2 (contracting/construction preparation): 3-5 years. Phase 3 (implementation): 5-7 years. Total: 10-15 years. This assumes selection of a proven reactor technology with an established licensing basis. For an unproven technology like MSR, add 5-10+ years for regulatory framework development, safety case preparation, and international peer review ([IAEA NG-G-3.1 Rev. 2, 2024](https://www.iaea.org/topics/infrastructure-development/milestones-approach)) (IAEA guidance, verified).

4. **Thorium MSR waste: potentially lower long-lived actinides, but unvalidated at scale** — The thorium fuel cycle produces 1,000-10,000x fewer transuranic actinides than the uranium-plutonium cycle, because 5 neutron captures are needed to produce transuranics from Th-232 vs 1 from U-238. MSR waste radiotoxicity could decay to natural uranium ore levels in ~300 years vs ~100,000 years for LWR spent fuel. However, these figures are theoretical — no thorium MSR has operated long enough to validate waste composition at equilibrium ([World Nuclear Association](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/current-and-future-generation/thorium); [OECD-NEA, 2015](https://www.oecd-nea.org/upload/docs/application/pdf/2019-12/7228-thorium-es.pdf)) (systematic review, moderate-confidence evidence).

5. **Proliferation risk: U-233 is weapons-usable despite U-232 contamination** — Thorium breeding produces U-233, which is weapons-usable (critical mass ~15kg). The U-232 contaminant (2.6-year half-life, hard gamma emitter) makes handling dangerous but does not make weapons construction impossible with state-level resources. MSRs with online chemical processing could separate Pa-233 (which decays to clean U-233), creating a proliferation pathway absent in solid-fuel reactors. The IAEA classifies U-233 as direct-use nuclear material ([IAEA](https://www.iaea.org/); [Wikipedia — Thorium fuel cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorium_fuel_cycle); [NRC NUREG/CR-7176](https://www.nrc.gov/docs/ML1405/ML14050A083.pdf)) (regulatory/academic analysis, high-confidence evidence).

6. **NRC Part 53 for advanced reactors not available until late 2025+** — The US NRC's technology-inclusive regulatory framework (10 CFR Part 53) mandated by NEIMA 2019 is still under development. The NRC issued a construction permit for the first MSR (Abilene Christian University's MSRR) in September 2024, but this is a research reactor, not a commercial power plant. No MSR has received a commercial operating license in any jurisdiction ([NRC](https://www.nrc.gov/about-nrc/regulatory/licensing); [ORNL/TM-2018/944](https://info.ornl.gov/sites/publications/Files/Pub114937.pdf)) (primary source, verified).

7. **A country without regulatory framework would need to adopt an existing framework** — Rather than developing a bespoke regulatory framework (10+ years), new nuclear countries typically adopt or adapt an existing framework (US NRC, French ASN, IAEA SSR-2/1). This only works for proven technologies with existing safety analyses. No thorium MSR safety analysis report (SAR) exists that could be adopted by reference, whereas AP1000 and EPR have complete SARs accepted by multiple regulators ([IAEA Milestones Approach](https://www-pub.iaea.org/MTCD/Publications/PDF/PUB2073_web.pdf)) (regulatory analysis, high-confidence evidence).

## Implementation Guidance

### Recommended Path: Gen III+ PWR with IAEA Technical Cooperation

1. **Year 1-2: IAEA engagement**
   - Request IAEA Integrated Nuclear Infrastructure Review (INIR) mission
   - Establish Nuclear Energy Programme Implementing Organization (NEPIO)
   - Begin Phase 1 of IAEA Milestones Approach (19 infrastructure issues)
   - Draft nuclear law and establish independent regulatory body

2. **Year 2-4: Technology selection and vendor engagement**
   - Issue Expression of Interest to qualified vendors: Westinghouse (AP1000), EDF (EPR2), KHNP (APR-1400), Rosatom (VVER-1200)
   - Conduct site characterization (seismic, hydrology, population density)
   - Negotiate intergovernmental agreement (IGA) with technology-providing country

3. **Year 4-7: Licensing and contracting**
   - Adopt regulatory framework by reference (recommend: IAEA SSR-2/1 + technology-providing country's framework)
   - Issue construction permit based on vendor's existing Safety Analysis Report
   - Sign EPC contract with finance (likely vendor-country export credit)

4. **Year 7-14: Construction and commissioning**
   - 6-8 year construction period (realistic for FOAK in new country)
   - IAEA operational safety review team (OSART) pre-operational mission
   - Progressive commissioning: fuel load → criticality → grid connection

## Alternatives Considered

### 1. Small Modular Reactor (SMR) Fleet — 10 x 100MW

A fleet of 10 SMRs (e.g., NuScale VOYGR, GE-Hitachi BWRX-300, Holtec SMR-300) totaling 1GW could offer modular construction, factory fabrication, and potentially faster deployment per unit. However, no SMR has yet achieved commercial operation (NuScale VOYGR cancelled in November 2023; BWRX-300 under construction in Canada, expected 2029). LCOE estimates for SMRs range from $80-130/MWh FOAK. **Choose SMRs when**: grid cannot absorb 1GW at once, construction workforce is limited, or you need distributed baseload across multiple sites. Timeline impact: similar to large PWR (15-20 years for first unit in new country).

### 2. Renewable + Battery Storage (Solar/Wind + Li-ion)

Solar PV at $30-50/MWh + 4-hour Li-ion storage at $150-250/kWh provides dispatchable power without nuclear licensing complexity. For 1GW firm capacity with 90% availability, you would need ~3GW solar + ~12GWh storage (cost: $6-10B). Timeline: 3-5 years. However, this does not provide true baseload (overnight, multi-day low solar events) without massive overbuilding. **Choose renewables+storage when**: you need power within 5 years, have excellent solar/wind resources, and can tolerate ~80-85% firm capacity factor.

### 3. Gas CCGT Bridge + Future Nuclear

A 1GW gas CCGT plant can be built in 3-4 years at $1,000-1,200/kW, providing immediate baseload at $50-70/MWh (depending on gas price). This serves as a bridge while developing nuclear infrastructure over 15+ years. Total cost: $1.2B (CCGT) + $6.2B (future PWR) = $7.4B, but with power available 12+ years sooner. Carbon emissions: ~350g CO2/kWh vs ~12g for nuclear. **Choose CCGT bridge when**: immediate power need cannot wait 15+ years, gas supply is secure, and carbon constraints are not binding.

## Adversarial Review

### Counterarguments

1. **"Thorium MSR waste advantages justify the wait"** — The theoretical waste profile is genuinely attractive (300 vs 100,000 years radiotoxicity). But this benefit is unvalidated at scale, and the country needs 1GW baseload power now, not in 25-40 years. Waste management for PWR spent fuel is a solved engineering problem (dry cask storage, geological disposal). The perfect should not be the enemy of the good.

2. **"China will commercialize thorium MSR by 2035"** — China's roadmap targets 100MW by 2035, still 10x below the required 1GW. Even if China succeeds, the technology would need adaptation, licensing, and construction in your country — adding another 10+ years. Realistic timeline for a non-Chinese country to deploy Chinese thorium MSR technology: 2045-2050 at earliest.

3. **"MSR has inherent safety advantages (negative temperature coefficient, atmospheric pressure)"** — These are real advantages, but they don't eliminate the regulatory burden. Novel safety features require novel safety analysis, novel regulatory acceptance criteria, and novel operational procedures — all of which take longer to develop and approve than proven PWR safety cases.

### Failure Modes

- **Political change during 15-year PWR timeline**: Nuclear programs span multiple election cycles; loss of political support mid-construction is the #1 risk
- **Vendor lock-in**: Choosing a single vendor (especially Rosatom) creates geopolitical dependency for 60+ years of fuel supply and maintenance
- **Cost escalation**: FOAK nuclear in a new country has historically exceeded budget by 50-200% (Olkiluoto 3, Flamanville 3, Vogtle 3&4)

## Recommendation

**Proceed with Gen III+ PWR (AP1000 or APR-1400) under the IAEA Milestones Approach. Do not pursue thorium MSR.** Confidence: 88%.

The thorium MSR is a research-stage technology with zero commercial-scale deployments, no available vendor, no existing safety analysis, and no regulatory framework in any country. For a country building its first nuclear program, the risk of adopting an unproven technology is unacceptable. A Gen III+ PWR offers proven safety, established licensing, multiple vendor options, and a realistic 15-20 year timeline.

**When this recommendation changes:**
- If a thorium MSR achieves commercial operation (100MW+) with full regulatory approval in any country: reassess for your second nuclear unit
- If the country's power need is 20+ years out: consider MSR alongside Gen IV options (sodium-cooled fast reactors, high-temperature gas reactors)
- If proliferation concerns are paramount: AP1000/APR-1400 with LEU fuel have established IAEA safeguards; thorium MSR safeguards are undefined
- If the country has massive thorium reserves (India, Brazil, Australia): long-term thorium strategy may complement initial PWR deployment
